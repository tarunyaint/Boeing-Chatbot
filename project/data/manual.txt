# **Foreword**

## **Purpose of the Manual**

This Aircraft Maintenance Manual (AMM) — in its training-document form — provides the maintenance technician with the procedures, illustrations, and reference data needed to perform routine line maintenance, scheduled inspections, and selected repairs on the Boeing 737-800 series airframe and its installed systems. The manual is written under the assumption that the user is a licensed maintenance professional working in a regulated maintenance environment, under the supervision of a Certifying Staff member or equivalent.

Maintenance instructions in this document follow the ATA iSpec 2200 numbering convention. Chapter numbers correspond to standard ATA chapter assignments (ATA 24 for electrical power, ATA 27 for flight controls, ATA 29 for hydraulic power, ATA 32 for landing gear, ATA 53 for fuselage, etc.) so that technicians familiar with one operator's documentation can navigate this manual without retraining.

## **Manual Hierarchy**

The 737-800 maintenance documentation set, in actual airline operation, consists of multiple coordinated documents. This synthetic training document references that hierarchy as follows:

- Aircraft Maintenance Manual (AMM): Line and base-maintenance procedures, servicing, and component removal and installation.

- Structural Repair Manual (SRM): Approved data for the repair of metallic and composite primary and secondary structure.

- Illustrated Parts Catalog (IPC): Part numbers, effectivity, and assembly relationships.

- Wiring Diagram Manual (WDM): Schematic and pictorial wiring diagrams, wire lists, and equipment lists.

- Fault Isolation Manual (FIM): Troubleshooting procedures keyed to flight-deck effects and maintenance messages.

- Component Maintenance Manual (CMM): Shop-level overhaul and bench-test procedures for line replaceable units (LRUs).

- Master Minimum Equipment List (MMEL) and Configuration Deviation List (CDL): Dispatch conditions for inoperative items.

- Service Bulletins (SB) and Airworthiness Directives (AD): Notifications of modifications, inspections, or corrective actions.

## **Approved Data**

In an actual maintenance environment, approved data is the only acceptable source for technical instructions. Approved data includes the manufacturer's current-revision manuals; ADs issued by the State of Design and the State of Registry; the operator's accepted maintenance program; and approved deviations such as Engineering Orders (EOs) or Repair Design Approval (RDA) packages.

Because this synthetic training document is by definition not approved data, any procedure described herein must not be used as a reference when working on an aircraft. The procedures are written in a realistic style purely so a conversational AI can be trained to recognize, reproduce, and answer questions about that style.

## **Acknowledgment of the Synthetic Nature of This Document**

Throughout this manual the reader will encounter part numbers, torque values, dimensional tolerances, inspection limits, and consumable references that look authentic but are entirely fabricated. The fabrication is deliberate: it provides realistic surface features for AI training without copying or restating proprietary manufacturer data. Where a number is needed for a procedure to be coherent, a plausible but invented value is used.

Three classes of advisory note are used throughout this manual. Each is set off in a bordered box and is positioned immediately before the step it qualifies. Technicians must read and comply with every WARNING and CAUTION as they are encountered.

**a.	**Loosen the four 1/4-turn fasteners.

**b.	**Carefully lower the panel; do not let it swing.

**2.	**Identify the affected component.

**a.	**IF the part number on the dataplate matches the IPC, continue to step 3.

**b.	**IF the part number does not match, stop work and contact the responsible engineer.

Effectivity defines the population of aircraft to which a procedure applies. ## **Sign-Off Discipline**

Each numbered step that requires verification carries a sign-off box in the operator's task card. The signature confirms that the technician performed the step as written, observed the inspection criteria, and accepts personal responsibility for that step. Pencil-whipping — signing for work that was not performed — is the single most common contributor to maintenance-induced events, and a regulatory violation. Sign only what you have personally completed.

# **Chapter 1 — General Information and Safety**

## **1.1 Scope and Applicability**

This chapter establishes the general technical and safety framework within which all procedures in this manual are performed. It applies to all maintenance personnel — including line technicians, hangar technicians, certifying staff, support personnel, and contracted personnel — who perform any task on the airframe, engines, components, or supporting equipment described in the remainder of this document.

The scope of this manual includes line maintenance (transit, daily, weekly, A check) and base maintenance (C check, structural inspections, modifications) up to the level customarily performed by an airline maintenance organization. Heavy structural repair, full-engine overhaul, and component shop work fall outside this scope and are addressed by the SRM and CMM documents respectively.

This manual is intended as a reference for aircraft built to the 737 Next-Generation production standard, in commercial passenger configuration. Cargo conversion (737-800BCF) effectivity is noted where applicable. Government and military variants are not covered.

## **1.2 Personnel Qualifications**

Maintenance activities described in this manual must be performed only by personnel who hold the appropriate license, type rating, authorization, and current task experience as required by the operator's accepted maintenance program and the regulatory framework of the State of Registry.

### **1.2.1 Licensed Maintenance Personnel**

Licensed maintenance personnel hold a current EASA Part-66 Category B1, B2, or C license, an FAA Airframe and Powerplant (A&P) certificate with Inspection Authorization (IA) as applicable, or the equivalent national license. The license must include the appropriate type rating for the Boeing 737-800 or be supported by formal type training. The licensed technician's signature on a maintenance record represents the legal authorization that the work was carried out in accordance with approved data.

### **1.2.2 Support and Specialized Personnel**

Certain tasks require specialized authorization beyond the basic license. These include:

- Non-destructive testing (NDT) — Level II or III qualification in the specific method (eddy current, ultrasonic, dye penetrant, magnetic particle, radiographic) per NAS 410 or EN 4179.

- Composite repair — Formal composite repair training plus on-the-job qualification, typically following SAE AS9100/CACRC guidelines.

- Welding — Welder certification per AWS D17.1 (aerospace fusion welding) or operator-specific equivalent, with regular recertification.

- Borescope inspection — Engine OEM-approved borescope training and current proficiency in the specific engine model.

- Engine ground run / taxi — Type-specific authorization that may include the cockpit-resource portion if the technician operates engines from the flight deck.

- Fuel tank entry — Confined space entry training, current physical fitness assessment, and rescue-team coverage for the duration of the entry.

### **1.2.3 Currency and Recency**

In addition to holding the appropriate license, the technician must be current: the operator's procedure manual will define a minimum number of tasks of a given category that must be performed within a rolling period (typically 24 months) for the technician to remain in currency. A technician who is out of currency must be supervised by a current technician until currency is re-established.

## **1.3 Safety Procedures**

The general safety practices below apply to every maintenance task. They are not repeated in each procedure, but the technician is responsible for applying them whenever the circumstances arise.

### **1.3.1 Aircraft Grounding**

| **WARNING** Before any maintenance activity that may generate or discharge static electricity — including fuel system work, oxygen system work, composite repair sanding, and avionics LRU exchange — verify that the aircraft is electrically bonded to the ramp grounding point. A floating aircraft on a dry ramp can hold sufficient static charge to ignite fuel vapors. |
| --- |

Use only an approved grounding cable. Connect the cable first to a permanent grounding point on the ramp (typically a bonded inlay in the concrete), then to a designated grounding receptacle on the aircraft — most commonly under the wing root fairing or at the nose-gear well. Verify continuity using a megger or bonding tester before commencing work.

### **1.3.2 Wheel Chocks and Parking Brake**

The aircraft must be chocked fore and aft on each main gear wheel before maintenance begins. The chocks must be of the correct size for the tire diameter, in serviceable condition, and positioned in firm contact with the tire. The parking brake is set only when brake-system pressure is at the normal operating value; setting the parking brake on a depressurized system produces no clamping force. After hydraulic system depressurization, chocks are the sole means of restraining the aircraft.

### **1.3.3 Working at Height**

Many 737-800 maintenance tasks take place on top of the wing, the upper fuselage, the horizontal stabilizer, or the engine. A fall from any of these surfaces is potentially fatal.

- Use only approved maintenance stands of the correct height and footprint for the area being worked on.

- Wear a full-body harness with a shock-absorbing lanyard tied off to an approved anchor when working on any surface higher than 1.8 m (6 ft) above the next lower level.

- Never use the leading edge of the wing, the antennas, the static wicks, the pitot probes, or the vortex generators as anchor points. These items are not load-rated for fall arrest and can be permanently damaged by even a few hundred newtons of side load.

- Approved anchor points are identified in the AMM by a small placard reading 'TIE-DOWN' or 'HARNESS ANCHOR' adjacent to the structural ring.

### **1.3.4 Engine and APU Hazard Zones**

Whenever an engine or the APU is running, intake and exhaust hazard zones are established around the aircraft and must be respected by all personnel. Approximate dimensions for the 737-800 at idle are summarized in the table below; the values increase substantially at takeoff thrust and must not be applied unmodified to ground runs above idle.

| **Engine Power Setting** | **Forward Intake Hazard Zone** | **Aft Exhaust Hazard Zone** | **Side Clearance** |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| APU running | Not applicable | 5 m (16 ft) | Not applicable |
| Engine ground idle | 4 m (13 ft) radius | 45 m (150 ft) | 5 m (16 ft) |
| Engine taxi/break-out | 5 m (16 ft) radius | 60 m (200 ft) | 8 m (26 ft) |
| Engine takeoff thrust | 9 m (30 ft) radius | 190 m (620 ft) | 18 m (60 ft) |

| **WARNING** Engine ingestion of a person standing within the intake hazard zone is invariably fatal. The hazard zone must be cleared and a fire-guard/spotter stationed before engine start. |

## **1.4 Lockout and Tagout (LOTO)**

Lockout and Tagout (LOTO) is the administrative and physical control of stored energy sources — electrical, hydraulic, pneumatic, mechanical, and chemical — during maintenance. LOTO is required whenever the unexpected release of energy could result in injury or equipment damage.

### **1.4.1 LOTO Procedure**

**1.	**Identify all energy sources that affect the work to be done. Common 737-800 sources include the three hydraulic systems (System A, System B, and Standby), the bleed-air system, the pneumatic accumulators, the battery bus, the AC and DC distribution buses, the static-port heat, and the rain repellent system.

**2.	**Notify all affected personnel — the flight deck, ramp, and any concurrent maintenance teams — that LOTO will be applied.

**3.	**Shut down the affected system through normal means (pulling the corresponding circuit breaker, depressurizing the hydraulic reservoir, isolating the bleed valve).

**4.	**Apply a physical lock to the isolating device. For circuit breakers, use a CB collar locked by a numbered LOTO padlock; for hydraulic shut-off valves, use a valve lockout clamp.

**5.	**Attach a tag to the lock that states the date, the technician's name and signature, and a brief description of the work. The tag is the documentation of the lockout.

**6.	**Verify zero-energy state: attempt to operate the affected system from its normal control. If the system does not respond, the LOTO is effective. If it responds, stop, troubleshoot, and re-apply LOTO.

**7.	**Perform the maintenance task.

**8.	**On completion, the same technician who applied the lock must remove it. Removal of another technician's LOTO lock requires the documented agreement of the duty supervisor.

| **CAUTION** A second technician must never remove a first technician's LOTO lock without explicit, documented authorization from the duty supervisor. Bypassing LOTO has caused multiple fatalities in commercial maintenance. |
| --- |

## **1.5 Hazardous Materials**

Aircraft maintenance involves routine handling of materials that are flammable, corrosive, toxic by ingestion or inhalation, sensitizing, carcinogenic, or any combination of these. Every chemical brought into the maintenance area must be accompanied by a current Safety Data Sheet (SDS) and must be handled in accordance with the SDS, the operator's hazardous-materials manual, and the regulatory requirements of the jurisdiction.

### **1.5.1 Common Hazardous Materials**

| **Material** | **Typical Use** | **Principal Hazards** | **Required PPE** |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Skydrol LD-4 (or equiv.) | Hydraulic fluid for Systems A, B, and Standby | Skin and eye irritant; degrades many paints and plastics | Nitrile gloves, splash goggles, apron |
| Jet A / Jet A-1 | Engine and APU fuel; tank entry contamination | Flammable; aspiration hazard; dermatitis on repeated contact | Nitrile gloves, FR coveralls, eye protection |
| Gaseous oxygen (GOX) | Crew and passenger emergency systems | Strong oxidizer; supports vigorous combustion of normally non-flammable materials when concentration is elevated | Oil-free clothing; no hydrocarbons within 6 m |
| Cadmium-plated fasteners | Legacy hardware; brazed assemblies | Cadmium fume is a Group 1 carcinogen if the fastener is heated or ground | Respirator with P3 cartridge; local extraction |
| Polysulfide sealants | Fuel-tank sealing; pressure-cabin sealing | Skin sensitizer; vapor irritant; specific cure conditions | Nitrile gloves, vapor respirator |
| Epoxy adhesives | Composite repair; bonded doublers | Skin sensitizer with risk of allergic dermatitis on repeated exposure | Nitrile gloves, eye protection |
| Methyl ethyl ketone (MEK) | Solvent wipe for paint preparation | Highly flammable; central nervous system depressant on inhalation | Solvent-resistant gloves, eye protection, ventilation |
| Lead-acid battery electrolyte | Main and standby battery (some configs) | Strongly corrosive; hydrogen gas generation during charging | Acid-resistant gloves, face shield, apron |

| **WARNING** Cadmium dust is a confirmed human carcinogen. Mechanical removal of cadmium plating — including grinding the head of a cadmium-plated fastener — must be performed only at a workstation equipped with local exhaust ventilation, by personnel wearing a P3 respirator. Never grind cadmium-plated parts in open hangar space. |

### **1.5.2 Spill Response**

Every maintenance area must have an accessible spill kit with absorbent for hydraulic fluid and fuel, neutralizer for battery electrolyte, and a sharps container for contaminated material. Small spills (< 1 L) of hydraulic fluid or fuel are absorbed immediately and the contaminated absorbent placed in a sealed metal drum for disposal as hazardous waste. Large spills require the duty environmental officer and may require ramp closure.

## **1.7 Tool Control and Calibration**

All tools used on the aircraft must be accounted for at the beginning and end of every task. A single un-accounted tool is treated as a Foreign Object Debris (FOD) incident and is investigated until the tool is located or its location is positively explained. Tools left in flight controls have caused control jams that ended in hull losses.

### **1.7.1 Tool Issue and Return**

Tools are issued from a controlled tool crib against the technician's badge. The technician confirms the count at issue, the count at return, and the condition of each tool. Damaged tools are tagged out of service and replaced. A 'tool short' at end of shift triggers the operator's lost-tool procedure: shadow-board check, area search, and — if the tool remains missing — a report to the technical-records office before the aircraft is returned to service.

### **1.7.2 Calibrated Tools**

Torque wrenches, pressure gauges, voltmeters, multimeters, micrometers, dial indicators, bonding testers, and any tool used to verify an acceptance criterion must be on a calibration register. Each calibrated tool carries a label showing the date of last calibration, the calibration interval (typically 12 months but as short as 6 months for high-use precision instruments), and the next due date.

| **CAUTION** A calibrated tool that is dropped — even from a workbench height — must be removed from service and re-verified before further use. Drop-recalibration is required even if no visible damage is evident. |
| --- |

## **1.8 Foreign Object Damage Prevention**

Foreign Object Damage (FOD) is damage caused by any item that should not be where it is. The most catastrophic FOD events involve loose hardware in an engine inlet (engine ingestion), in a fuel tank (fuel-flow obstruction or arcing), or in a control surface (jam). FOD prevention is everyone's responsibility, not just the duty FOD walker's.

### **1.8.1 Sources of FOD**

- Hardware: loose fasteners, washers, nuts dropped during installation.

- Lock-wire ends: short cuts of lock-wire that fall into the work area.

- Personal items: pens, coins, ID-card lanyards, jewelry.

- Consumables: rivet stems, drill swarf, sanding dust, cured sealant flash.

- Tools: small bits — Allen keys, drill bits, taps — that roll into recesses.

- Packaging: shrink wrap, plastic caps from new components, vendor tags.

### **1.8.2 FOD Control Practices**

**1.	**Empty all chest pockets before climbing onto the aircraft. Use tethered tools when working over open fuel tanks, engine cores, or flight-control bays.

**2.	**Cap or plug open lines, ports, and ducts the moment they are disconnected. Use marked, accountable plugs (red or yellow) that are listed on the task card. Each plug installed is recorded; each plug removed is recorded.

**3.	**Cut lock-wire over a clean towel or a magnetic mat. Count short ends before leaving the area.

**4.	**Use shadow-board tool bags rather than open trays inside the aircraft.

**5.	**Perform a 'clean as you go' practice. Do not accumulate swarf, sealant flash, or paper. Vacuum or wipe after every step that generates debris.

**6.	**Perform a final FOD walk before closing any access panel. Look, feel by hand inside recesses, and use an inspection mirror with a light to view areas the eye cannot reach directly.

| **NOTE** Borescope inspection of engine inlets is required following any task that opens the engine, the inlet cowl, or the pylon. The borescope check verifies that no fastener or tool fragment was left in the gas path. |

# **Chapter 2 — Standard Practices (ATA 20)**

ATA Chapter 20 collects practices that apply to maintenance and repair across the airframe, regardless of the specific zone or system being worked on. Mastery of standard practices is the foundation of consistent, repeatable workmanship. The procedures in later chapters all assume that the technician will follow the standard practices established here.

## **2.1 Fastener Installation**

The 737-800 airframe uses a wide range of mechanical fasteners: solid rivets, blind rivets, Hi-Lok pin-and-collar fasteners, Hi-Tigue interference fasteners, lockbolts, threaded bolts (hex, 12-point, and torx), nuts (self-locking, castellated, and plain), and dedicated panel fasteners (1/4-turn Camloc and Dzus). Each fastener type has a defined installation method, inspection criterion, and removal method.

### **2.1.1 Solid Rivets**

Solid rivets are the workhorse joining element for aluminum airframe construction. The 737-800 fuselage skin is held to the stringers and frames by 5/32-inch and 3/16-inch 2117-T4 'AD' rivets countersunk and flush. Solid rivets are upset cold by impact (rivet gun and bucking bar) or pressure (squeeze riveter).

#### ***Installation Steps***

**1.	**Confirm the rivet specification by reading the head marking. AD-series 2117 rivets are marked with a dimple in the center of the head. DD-series 2024 rivets are marked with a raised pip.

**2.	**Confirm the rivet grip length. The grip is the unupset length between the underside of the manufactured head and the start of the formed tail. The grip required is the joint thickness plus 1.5 times the rivet shank diameter, rounded up to the next dash number.

**3.	**Insert the rivet into the prepared hole, manufactured head against the dimpled or counter-sunk side of the joint.

**4.	**Position the bucking bar squarely against the rivet tail.

**5.	**Apply the rivet gun in line with the rivet axis. Drive the rivet with short bursts. Maintain bar pressure throughout.

**6.	**Inspect the formed (shop) head against the acceptance criteria in the table below.

| **Inspection Criterion** | **Acceptable** | **Reject** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Shop head diameter | 1.5 × shank diameter | Less than 1.4 × or more than 1.6 × shank diameter |
| Shop head height | 0.5 × shank diameter | Less than 0.4 × or more than 0.6 × shank diameter |
| Manufactured head | Flush within ±0.004 in. (countersunk) | Proud or sunken beyond limit; clipped head |
| Tilt | Shop head axis within 5° of normal | More than 5° tilt |
| Cracks | None visible at 10× magnification | Any radial or circumferential crack |
| Smile / lateral mark | None on aluminum skin surface | Bucking-bar smile on skin in primary structure |

| **WARNING** Driving a rivet with insufficient bucking-bar pressure produces a clinched joint that may pass visual inspection but is significantly weaker in fatigue. The bucking bar must remain firmly against the tail through the entire upset. |
| --- |

### **2.1.2 Hi-Lok Pin-and-Collar Fasteners**

Hi-Lok fasteners are a two-piece pin-and-collar system used where higher strength than a solid rivet is required and where the back side of the joint is accessible. The Hi-Lok pin is installed in interference or transition fit; the threaded collar is torqued to a designed break-off shank, which separates when the correct preload is reached. The remaining hex socket in the pin head is for reaction during collar installation.

#### ***Installation Steps***

**1.	**Confirm the Hi-Lok pin part number (HL11, HL12, HL18, HL19, HL70 are commonly used) and the collar part number (HL70-, HL75-, HL80- series). The pin diameter, head type (flush or protruding), and grip length must match the joint.

**2.	**Lubricate the threads of the pin with the operator's approved fastener lubricant before installation. The lubricant ensures a consistent break-off torque.

**3.	**Insert the pin into the prepared hole. The shank should be a press fit; a 'tap fit' indicates an oversize hole — stop and consult the engineering disposition.

**4.	**From the opposite side, hand-thread the collar onto the pin until contact is felt with the substructure.

**5.	**Apply the Hi-Lok installation tool: the inner Allen socket engages the pin head, the outer hex socket engages the collar. The tool reacts the rotation between the two so the pin does not turn during collar drive.

**6.	**Run the collar until the break-off shank separates. Capture the broken shank and verify count at end of task.

**7.	**Inspect the installed fastener: the residual collar should leave one to three full threads visible above the wrenching shank, the pin head should be flush with the dimpled surface, and there must be no rotation between pin and joint.

| **CAUTION** Hi-Lok pins are designed for one installation only. A Hi-Lok pin that has been backed out, even partially, must not be reinstalled. The interference fit is unrecoverable once the pin has been displaced. |
| --- |

### **2.1.3 Blind Fasteners**

Blind fasteners (Cherrymax, Cherrylock, NAS1399, A&P blind rivets, etc.) are used where only one side of the joint is accessible. The blind fastener forms a head on the inaccessible (blind) side during installation. Blind fasteners are typically permitted only on secondary structure or as a specific repair approved by the SRM; primary-structure use requires engineering approval.

#### ***Installation Steps for a CR3213 Cherrymax Fastener (Illustrative)***

**1.	**Confirm the Cherrymax specification by reading the dash number on the head and verifying grip length per the IPC.

**2.	**Insert the fastener into the prepared hole, ensuring the head seats flush against the dimple or countersink.

**3.	**Engage the pulling head of the installation tool over the stem.

**4.	**Activate the tool. The stem will draw, the driving anvil will form the blind head, and the stem will fracture flush with the manufactured head when the correct preload is reached.

**5.	**Inspect the installation: the stem fracture should be flush within ±0.020 in. with the head; the head should be flush and unmarked; no daylight should be visible at the head-to-skin interface.

| **NOTE** Cherrymax stems are retained by a locking collar; the broken stem does not fall out of the fastener. The flush stem is acceptance evidence of correct installation. |
| --- |

## **2.2 Torque Application**

Correct torque is the foundation of a reliable threaded joint. Under-torque leaves the joint vulnerable to fatigue loosening; over-torque can yield the bolt or strip the female thread. Every torque value in this manual is given as a range — the installation must fall within the range, not at one end.

### **2.2.1 Torque Wrench Selection**

Select a torque wrench whose mid-range is closest to the target torque. A torque wrench is most accurate at the center of its scale (typically ±4%) and least accurate at the bottom (often ±10% in the lowest 10% of the range).

- For target torque ≤ 5 N·m (44 in-lb): use a 1–10 N·m precision wrench.

- For target torque 5–25 N·m (44–220 in-lb): use a 5–25 N·m wrench.

- For target torque 25–100 N·m: use a 20–100 N·m wrench.

- For target torque > 100 N·m: use a 1/2-inch-drive wrench in the 50–200 N·m range or larger.

### **2.2.2 Application of Torque**

**1.	**Verify that the wrench calibration is current. Note the wrench serial number on the task card.

**2.	**Set the torque value before starting work. Click-type wrenches are set to the desired value and locked; dial-type wrenches read the value directly during application.

**3.	**Confirm that the threaded joint is clean, dry, and free of burrs unless the procedure specifically calls for lubricated threads. A 'wet' torque (lubricated) is typically 75% of the 'dry' (unlubricated) value to produce the same preload.

**4.	**Apply the torque in a single continuous motion until the wrench clicks or the dial indicates the target value. Avoid jerky motion.

**5.	**For multi-fastener patterns (engine mounts, wheel hubs, gearbox housings), torque in the cross-pattern sequence shown in the IPC. Apply torque in two or three stages — typically 30%, 60%, and 100% of final torque — to seat the joint evenly.

**6.	**After the final torque, mark the bolt and the adjacent structure with a single torque-seal stripe. A torque-seal stripe that has shifted between successive inspections is a positive indication that the joint has rotated.

| **NOTE** The torque-seal stripe is for inspection only and is not the means of retention. If the fastener relies on lock-wire, cotter pin, or self-locking nut for retention, that mechanism must still be installed. |
| --- |

| **CAUTION** Never use a torque wrench to break a fastener loose. Torque wrenches are calibrated for tightening only; breaking a frozen bolt with a torque wrench can damage the internal mechanism and silently shift the calibration. |
| --- |

## **2.3 Sealant Application and Cure**

Polysulfide and polythioether sealants are used on the 737-800 for three principal duties: (a) fuel-tank sealing, where the sealant retains Jet A in the integral wing tanks and the fuselage center tank; (b) pressure-cabin sealing, around penetrations, doors, and the upper-lobe joints; and (c) aerodynamic sealing, where a smooth fillet over a fastener row reduces drag and prevents wind-driven moisture ingress.

### **2.3.1 Sealant Classes and Applications**

| **Class** | **Typical Designation** | **Application Time** | **Tack-Free** | **Cure Time** | **Use** |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Class A | PR-1422 A-1/2 | 30 min | 8 h | 24 h | Brush coat / faying surface |
| Class B | PR-1422 B-2 | 2 h | 24 h | 72 h | Fillet seal, large repairs |
| Class B-4 | PR-1828 B-4 | 4 h | 48 h | 120 h | Long open-time fillet |
| Class C | PR-1773 C-20 | 20 min | 4 h | 12 h | Fast-cure access panel |
| High-T | PR-1750 B-2 | 2 h | 24 h | 96 h | Engine pylon / firewall |

| **NOTE** Application time is the working life from the moment of mix until the sealant is no longer placeable. Tack-free time is the time after which the sealant skin is firm to the touch. Cure time is the time to attain full mechanical and chemical properties at the standard cure condition of 25°C and 50% relative humidity. |
| --- |

### **2.3.2 Sealant Application Procedure**

**1.	**Verify the SEMKIT or two-part kit lot number, expiry date, and storage condition. Refrigerated kits must reach room temperature before mixing.

**2.	**Activate the SEMKIT by removing the divider rod and mixing the base and accelerator using the supplied rod. Mix until the colour is uniform — typically a uniform grey, blue, or tan depending on the product. Mixing time is normally 90 to 120 seconds.

**3.	**Transfer the activated sealant to the dispensing cartridge if not already in cartridge form.

**4.	**Prepare the substrate. Sealant must be applied to clean, dry, freshly abraded primer or bare alclad. Solvent-wipe with MEK or operator-approved solvent and follow the two-rag procedure: wet wipe with the first rag, dry wipe with the second clean rag before solvent evaporates.

**5.	**Apply the sealant. For a fillet seal, the bead must be continuous and free of air entrapment. Press the bead into the joint with a tongue depressor or PTFE-coated spatula to ensure substrate contact.

**6.	**Tool the sealant smooth. Use the operator's tool kit; do not use bare fingers — finger contamination compromises adhesion.

**7.	**Record the application date, kit lot, expiry, and operator initials in the maintenance record. The earliest acceptable return-to-service is governed by the cure time at the actual ambient temperature, not the standard cure time.

| **WARNING** Sealant fumes are an inhalation hazard and a known skin sensitizer. Work only in a well-ventilated area, change gloves frequently, and never re-use gloves between different sealant kits. |
| --- |

### **2.3.3 Cure Time and Re-Service**

Cure time is highly temperature-dependent. The rule of thumb for polysulfide sealants is that the cure rate doubles for every 10°C increase in temperature, within the manufacturer's allowed window. Conversely, work in a cold hangar at 10°C may double the cure time. The operator's procedure manual contains a temperature-vs-cure chart for each sealant class; this manual reproduces a synthetic version in Appendix A.

## **2.4 Corrosion Prevention and Control**

Corrosion is the chemical or electrochemical degradation of metal in response to its environment. Aluminum alloys used in the 737-800 (2024-T3, 7075-T6, 7050-T7451, 7475-T761) are highly susceptible to galvanic corrosion when in contact with stainless steel, titanium, or graphite composites in the presence of moisture. The Corrosion Prevention and Control Programme (CPCP) prescribed by the operator's Maintenance Steering Group document (MSG-3 analysis) establishes corrosion inspection levels (CIL) for every zone of the aircraft.

### **2.4.1 Forms of Corrosion Encountered**

- Surface corrosion: General oxidation of the alloy surface; the most common and least serious form. Removed by light abrasion and re-primed.

- Pitting corrosion: Localized attack producing discrete pits, typically initiated at chloride deposits. Pits deeper than the protective clad layer must be evaluated for fastener edge distance compliance.

- Intergranular corrosion: Attack along the grain boundaries of the alloy, often invisible from the surface. Indicates significant property loss in 7075-T6 and is cause for material replacement.

- Exfoliation: Advanced intergranular corrosion in rolled product; the metal delaminates in onion-skin layers. Confined almost exclusively to 7075 and 2024 in long-time service.

- Galvanic corrosion: Attack of the more anodic metal in a coupled pair. Aluminum is anodic to steel, titanium, and carbon composite; isolation by primer, sealant, or polysulfide tape is required at every dissimilar-metal interface.

- Filiform corrosion: Worm-like trails under paint, driven by moisture infiltration through a defect in the paint. Common on lower-fuselage skin.

- Stress corrosion cracking (SCC): Sub-critical crack growth under sustained tensile stress in a corrosive environment. 7075-T6 in the short-transverse direction is highly susceptible; SCC is a leading cause of structural-component retirement.

### **2.4.2 Corrosion Removal — Aluminum**

**1.	**Identify the extent of the corrosion. Mark the boundary with a soft pencil.

**2.	**Mechanically remove paint and primer from the affected area plus a 10 mm (0.4 in.) overlap. Use aluminum oxide cloth or a Scotch-Brite roloc pad of appropriate grit; never use steel wool or a wire brush on aluminum — embedded particles will initiate further corrosion.

**3.	**Remove the corrosion product itself by progressive blending. Stop frequently to measure the remaining material thickness with an ultrasonic thickness gauge or, where access permits, a depth micrometer.

**4.	**Compare the remaining thickness against the negative tolerance allowed by the SRM for that part. If the negative tolerance is exceeded, the part is not repairable by blend-out; engineering disposition is required.

**5.	**Apply chemical conversion coating (Alodine 1200S or operator-approved equivalent) to the bare aluminum. Allow the coating to develop for 1 to 3 minutes, then rinse with deionized water and dry.

**6.	**Apply primer (typically a strontium chromate-free epoxy primer) within 4 hours of the conversion coating. Recoat with topcoat per the operator's livery scheme.

| **WARNING** Strontium chromate primers contain hexavalent chromium, a confirmed human carcinogen. The operator should be transitioning to chromate-free primers (e.g., magnesium-rich primers) where qualified. Where chromate primer remains in use, full respiratory protection and skin protection are mandatory. |
| --- |

## **2.5 Composite Material Handling**

The 737-800 uses carbon-fiber-reinforced polymer (CFRP), fiberglass, and Kevlar in numerous secondary-structure parts: flight-control surfaces, fairings, radome, wing-to-body fairing, engine nacelle composite skins, and the floor panels. Composite repair is a specialized discipline requiring formal training; this section addresses only the general handling practices that any technician must follow when working in or near composite parts.

### **2.5.1 Composite Damage Categories**

| **Category** | **Damage Type** | **Typical Disposition** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Category 1 | Allowable damage; not detectable by routine inspection | No action; fly indefinitely. |
| Category 2 | Damage detectable by inspection within scheduled visits; not affecting flight safety | Repair at next opportunity per SRM. |
| Category 3 | Damage requiring readily detectable timely repair; immediate flight not affected | Repair within MEL/CDL allowable period. |
| Category 4 | Discrete-source damage (lightning, hail, impact); aircraft known to be damaged | Inspect and repair before flight. |
| Category 5 | Severe damage beyond repair limits | Replace component. |

### **2.5.2 Handling Practices**

- Composite parts do not show damage the way metal does. A barely visible impact damage (BVID) on the surface can mask significant internal delamination. Treat every reported impact as a Category 3 event until inspection demonstrates otherwise.

- Never set a metallic tool on a composite skin. The localized contact stress can initiate a fiber-matrix break.

- When drilling composite, use only diamond-coated or carbide drills designed for the material. High-speed steel drills tear the matrix and produce sub-surface delamination.

- Capture all drilling and sanding dust at the source. Carbon-fiber dust is electrically conductive and a respiratory irritant; it can short-circuit electronics and is a skin sensitizer.

- Composite parts must be stored on dedicated padded dollies. Edge contact with concrete or steel rack will damage the laminate edge.

| **CAUTION** Sanding cured composite generates conductive dust that can enter electronic enclosures and bridge low-voltage circuits. Mask off all openings near a composite sanding area and vacuum the work zone before opening any equipment bay. |
| --- |

### **2.5.3 Inspection Methods**

Three methods are routinely used to inspect composite for sub-surface damage:

- Coin-tap inspection: A coin or dedicated tap-tester is bounced lightly over the laminate. A dull thud (versus a sharp ring) indicates a delamination or core damage in honeycomb.

- Ultrasonic A-scan or C-scan: Pulse-echo ultrasonic mapping of the laminate; provides quantitative depth and area information.

- Thermographic inspection: Infrared imaging of the laminate after a brief thermal pulse; delaminations cool at a different rate than sound material and appear as features in the thermal image.

| **NOTE** Coin-tap is a screening method only. A positive coin-tap finding is followed by ultrasonic or thermographic confirmation before a repair disposition is made. |
| --- |

# **Chapter 3 — Fuselage Repair (ATA 53)**

ATA Chapter 53 covers the primary fuselage structure: the skin, frames, stringers, bulkheads, floor beams, doorframes, and window cutouts. The 737-800 fuselage is a stressed-skin semi-monocoque assembly fabricated principally from 2024-T3 clad aluminum skin riveted to extruded 7075-T6 stringers and rolled 2024 frames. Repairs in this chapter address damage that can be returned to service in line or hangar maintenance using methods described in the SRM.

## **3.1 Fuselage Inspection**

**3.1 Reference Information**

| **Item** | **Detail** |
| --- | --- |
| Effectivity | YA001 — YA999 (all 737-800 variants) |
| Reason for the Job | Routine inspection of fuselage primary structure per Maintenance Planning Document |
| Zones | Zones 100, 200, 300, 400 (forward, mid, aft fuselage) |
| Personnel Required | Two technicians (one inside, one outside); NDT Level II if findings require follow-up |
| Elapsed Time | Approximately 8 hours for full external visual; 16 hours including internal |

### **3.1.1 Job Set-Up**

Before beginning inspection, prepare the aircraft and the inspection area:

**1.	**Park the aircraft in a covered hangar position with adequate lighting (minimum 500 lux at the work surface).

**2.	**Wash the exterior fuselage to remove dirt, exhaust soot, and de-icing fluid residue. Inspection of a dirty surface is unreliable.

**3.	**Configure access stands to permit a 0.3 m (12 in.) standoff for visual inspection of every skin square.

**4.	**Issue a 10× hand magnifier, an inspection mirror, a flashlight, a depth gauge, and a roll of low-tack masking tape to each inspector.

**5.	**Lay out the inspection record sheets. Use the operator's fuselage map showing every skin section, lap-joint, butt-joint, and doubler.

### **3.1.2 Inspection Procedure**

| **NOTE** Inspect systematically — forward to aft, top to bottom, left side then right — so that no panel is skipped or duplicated. Mark every finding on the fuselage map with a unique reference (e.g., S-12, S-13, S-14) and a brief description. |
| --- |

**1.	**Inspect the skin for visible cracks, with particular attention to:

**a.	**Rivet rows on lap joints and butt joints — fatigue cracks commonly initiate at the upper rivet row of a longitudinal lap joint.

**b.	**Window cutout corners — local stress concentrations make these locations crack-prone.

**c.	**Door cutout corners — particularly the lower aft corners of the forward and aft passenger doors.

**d.	**Wing-to-body fairing terminations on the fuselage skin.

**2.	**Inspect every skin square for impact damage. Categorize each finding as:

**a.	**Scratch — material removed without local deformation.

**b.	**Dent — local deformation of the skin without material loss.

**c.	**Gouge — material removal accompanied by local deformation, indicating energetic contact.

**d.	**Puncture — material is locally absent and the substructure is exposed.

**3.	**Measure each finding. For dents, record:

**a.	**Length L (long-axis), width W (short-axis), and depth D from the original skin surface.

**b.	**Approximate position relative to nearest frame, stringer, or window.

**4.	**Inspect lap joints with a 10× magnifier for any indication of skin disbond or pillowing — bulging of one skin layer relative to the other, a visual indicator of internal corrosion in the lap.

**5.	**Inspect external paint coverage. Bare metal, peeling paint, or filiform corrosion under paint should be marked for follow-up.

**6.	**Photograph every finding with a scale rule visible in the frame. Save photographs to the operator's image-management system using the unique reference assigned to each finding.

## **3.2 Damage Classification**

The SRM divides damage into categories that drive the disposition. The categories used in this synthetic document are illustrative and follow the general industry convention:

| **Category** | **Definition** | **Disposition** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Allowable | Below the negative tolerance threshold of the SRM; no repair required. | Record only; continue in service. |
| Repair A | Cosmetic damage; structural function intact. | Touch-up paint; record. |
| Repair B | Local damage requiring blend-out and possibly a doubler. | Blend-out per SRM and re-protect. |
| Repair C | Damage requiring an external doubler or flush patch. | Doubler installation per Section 3.3 or flush patch per Section 3.4. |
| Repair D | Damage extending through the skin to substructure. | Engineering disposition; may require frame or stringer repair. |
| Replace | Damage outside any defined repair category. | Replace the affected skin panel, frame, or stringer assembly. |

## **3.3 External Doubler Installation**

An external doubler is the most common skin repair: a piece of compatible aluminum sheet is fastened over the damaged area, transferring load around the damage. The doubler must overlap the damage by a defined margin and use a fastener pattern that matches the parent-skin load path. The procedure below describes the installation of a representative external doubler over a Category-C dent.

**3.3 Reference Information**

| **Item** | **Detail** |
| --- | --- |
| Effectivity | All 737-800 |
| Reason for the Job | Repair of Category-C dent in fuselage skin |
| Zones | Zone 230 (representative) |
| Personnel Required | One technician plus inspector; NDT support |
| Elapsed Time | Approximately 12 hours including primer cure |
| References | SRM 53-30-01 (synthetic), AMM 51-21-04 |

### **3.3.1 Tools and Materials**

| **Item** | **Designation** | **Quantity** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Doubler material | 2024-T3 clad aluminum, 0.063 in. thick | As required to suit damage |
| Solid rivets | MS20426AD5 (5/32 in. flush head) | Per fastener pattern (synthetic) |
| Sealant | PR-1422 B-2 (Class B) | 1 SEMKIT per repair |
| Primer | BMS 10-11 (chrome-free epoxy) | 1 quart |
| Drill | Pneumatic drill with depth stop | 1 |
| Drill bits | #20, #11, #10, #4 cobalt | 1 set |
| Countersink cage | Microstop countersink, 100° | 1 |
| Rivet gun | 3X pneumatic riveter, mushroom set | 1 |
| Bucking bars | Various profiles to suit interior access | 1 set |
| Hole gauges | Pin gauges 0.1280-0.2010 in. | 1 set |
| Solvent wipe materials | MEK and lint-free wipes | As required |

### **3.3.2 Procedure**

**1.	**Mark the damage boundary on the skin with a soft graphite pencil. Add an overlap of one and a half times the maximum damage dimension, but not less than 25 mm (1.0 in.), in each direction. The marked rectangle defines the minimum doubler footprint.

**2.	**Cut the doubler from 2024-T3 clad sheet. Doubler corners are rounded to a radius of at least 12 mm (0.5 in.) — square corners produce stress concentrations and may initiate cracking in the doubler itself.

**3.	**Lay out the fastener pattern on the doubler. The pattern matches the local skin pattern: typical fuselage skin rivets are on 25 mm (1.0 in.) pitch in the longitudinal direction and 32 mm (1.25 in.) pitch in the circumferential direction. The doubler will have at least two rows of fasteners around its entire perimeter.

| **CAUTION** Maintain an edge distance of at least 2 × fastener diameter (2D) from each fastener centerline to the doubler edge. Edge distance less than 2D is a structural reject. |
| --- |

**4.	**Drill pilot holes (#40 drill, 0.098 in.) through the doubler at every fastener location.

**5.	**Position the doubler over the damage. Use shear-resistant 'Cleco' temporary fasteners through alternate pilot holes to clamp the doubler to the skin.

**6.	**Drill the pilot holes through the underlying skin, then progressively enlarge the holes to final diameter (#11 drill for a 5/32-inch rivet, producing a 0.190-in. hole). Use cobalt drills and a sharp bit; dull drills tear the surrounding material and oversize the hole.

**7.	**Deburr both sides of every hole. Hand-cycle a deburring tool one to two turns from each side — heavier deburring removes too much material and degrades the joint.

**8.	**Countersink the doubler holes to 100° to match the rivet head. Set the microstop countersink to produce a flush fit; verify on a sacrificial coupon before working on the doubler.

**9.	**Remove the doubler from the skin, retaining the Clecos in a tray.

**10.	**Apply faying-surface sealant to the underside of the doubler (the side that mates with the skin). The faying-surface seal is a brush coat of PR-1422 A-1/2 (Class A) or, where the repair is in a fuel-tank area, a wet-install sealant. Spread an even film 0.005–0.010 in. thick over the entire footprint.

**11.	**Reposition the doubler on the skin within the working life of the sealant. Re-install the Clecos in alternate holes.

**12.	**Install solid rivets in every hole, starting from the center of the doubler and working outward in a symmetrical pattern. This sequence ensures any sealant squeeze-out is expelled, not trapped in the joint.

| **WARNING** Do not install all the rivets on one edge before starting the opposite edge. Asymmetric installation can lock in a stress that the doubler will carry permanently. |
| --- |

**13.	**Tool a fillet seal of PR-1422 B-2 around the entire perimeter of the doubler, sealing the doubler-to-skin step. Pressurize the fillet against the joint with a tongue depressor; air entrapment is a leading cause of fillet-seal failure.

**14.	**Allow the sealant to reach tack-free condition (24 hours nominal) before applying primer and topcoat.

**15.	**Inspect the completed repair. Acceptance criteria are listed in the table below.

| **Criterion** | **Acceptable** | **Reject** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Rivet installation | All rivets meet shop-head criteria of Section 2.1.1 | Any clinched, tilted, or cracked rivet |
| Faying-surface sealant | Continuous bead visible at perimeter; no voids | Discontinuous seal or visible air paths |
| Doubler fit-up | Doubler in firm contact with skin; no daylight | Any gap > 0.005 in. before fastening |
| Edge distance | ≥ 2D on all fasteners | < 2D at any location |
| Pillowing / oil-canning | Doubler flat to ±0.020 in. across centre | Convex or concave bow |

## **3.4 Flush Patch Repair**

Where the airworthiness or aerodynamics require a flush exterior surface — for example, on the upper wing surface or in radar coverage areas — an external doubler is unacceptable. A flush patch is installed by cutting out the damaged skin and replacing it with a flush insert backed by an internal doubler. The flush patch requires more access and more time than an external doubler but restores the original skin contour.

### **3.4.1 Flush Patch Procedure (Summary)**

**1.	**Trim the damage out of the parent skin in a rectangular cut-out. Rounded corners (radius ≥ 12 mm) are mandatory.

**2.	**Fabricate an internal doubler from 2024-T3, sized to extend at least 25 mm beyond the cut-out on every side.

**3.	**Fabricate the flush patch (also called a 'plug') from skin material matching the parent skin gauge and alloy.

**4.	**Drill the joint pattern through the parent skin, the patch, and the internal doubler simultaneously, clamped in stack.

**5.	**Disassemble, deburr, and apply faying-surface sealant on both interfaces (skin-to-doubler and patch-to-doubler).

**6.	**Reassemble using countersunk Hi-Lok fasteners or solid rivets that produce a flush exterior.

**7.	**Tool a thin perimeter fillet at the patch-to-skin step, sand smooth after cure, and refinish to match the parent surface.

| **NOTE** The aerodynamic step at the patch-to-skin interface, after final sand and paint, should not exceed 0.005 in. (0.13 mm) for any patch on the wing upper surface, the fuselage forward of the wing, or any radar-significant area. |
| --- |

## **3.5 Stringer Repair**

Stringers run longitudinally along the fuselage interior, bonded and fastened to the inside of the skin. They are typically Z-section or hat-section extrusions in 7075-T6. Damage to a stringer (crack, dent, corrosion-related loss) is a higher-severity event than skin damage because the stringer carries axial load and contributes to the panel buckling stability.

### **3.5.1 Splice Repair of a Z-Stringer**

**1.	**Access the affected stringer through the cabin liner or cargo bay liner. Some areas may require removal of insulation blankets.

**2.	**Trim out the damaged stringer length. The cut is square to the stringer axis; the cut must be at least 50 mm clear of any frame intersection.

**3.	**Fabricate a splice section from the same 7075-T6 Z-section extrusion. Length = trimmed gap + 4 × stringer height on each side.

**4.	**Fabricate two splice doublers from 7075-T6 sheet, one for the flange and one for the web.

**5.	**Drill the splice fastener pattern in stack; the typical pattern is two rows of three Hi-Lok HL18-5 (5/32 in.) fasteners on each side of the splice joint.

**6.	**Apply faying-surface sealant to all mating interfaces.

**7.	**Install Hi-Lok fasteners, torque the collars, and verify the break-off.

**8.	**Restore corrosion protection: prime the spliced area with chrome-free primer; do not paint internal stringer surfaces beyond what the SRM allows.

**9.	**Re-install insulation blankets and cabin or cargo liner.

# **Chapter 4 — Wing Structure Repair (ATA 57)**

ATA Chapter 57 covers the wing primary structure: front and rear spars, upper and lower skin panels, ribs, stringers, leading-edge slats and their tracks, trailing-edge flap structure, and the wing-to-fuselage attachment. The 737-800 wing is a two-spar structure with integral fuel tanks between the spars from the wing root to the outboard tank end. The upper skin is the highest-loaded part of the wing in cruise flight, carrying compressive load; the lower skin carries tensile load. Repair limits differ accordingly.

## **4.1 Wing Inspection**

**4.1 Reference Information**

| **Item** | **Detail** |
| --- | --- |
| Effectivity | All 737-800 |
| Reason for the Job | Scheduled wing structural inspection (C check) |
| Zones | Zones 500, 600 (left and right wing) |
| Personnel Required | Two technicians plus NDT Level II eddy-current operator |
| Elapsed Time | Approximately 24 hours per wing |

### **4.1.1 External Visual Inspection**

**1.	**Inspect the upper skin from the leading edge to the trailing edge, root to tip. Pay particular attention to:

**a.	**Fastener rows on the upper skin attachment to the spar caps.

**b.	**Stringer-to-skin attachment lines.

**c.	**Vortex generator base plates (look for skin damage from missing or loose VGs).

**d.	**Inspection panel boundaries — fretting at the panel edge is common.

**2.	**Inspect the lower skin. The lower wing skin is in tension during 1-g flight and is the more fatigue-critical surface.

**a.	**Sealant fillet condition at every panel-to-panel joint.

**b.	**Drain hole patency at the most aft underbelly of the wing root fairing.

**c.	**Fuel-leak indications: blue staining ('blueing') of paint near a fastener row indicates Jet A weeping past a fastener seal.

**3.	**Inspect leading-edge slats, slat tracks, and slat fairings:

**a.	**Slat skin damage from bird strikes, hail, or runway debris.

**b.	**Slat track operating roller condition (visible through the slat-track inspection hole).

**c.	**Slat-to-leading-edge gap when fully retracted — should be even across the span.

**4.	**Inspect trailing-edge flaps and flap tracks. Flap tracks are highly loaded during takeoff and landing; track-roller wear and track-lining wear are inspected at every C check.

### **4.1.2 Internal Inspection (Fuel-Tank Entry)**

| **WARNING** Fuel-tank entry is a confined-space activity. A trained, certified tank-entry team — two persons inside, two persons outside as standby — is required. Continuous atmospheric monitoring (LEL, oxygen, hydrocarbon) is mandatory. A rescue harness, retrieval line, and self-contained breathing apparatus must be staged and ready at the tank access. |
| --- |

Fuel-tank internal inspection requires defueling, ventilation, and atmospheric clearance before personnel enter. The detailed procedure is given in AMM 28-11-00; this section addresses only structural inspection points visible from inside the tank.

- Spar webs: inspect for cracks at the spar-to-rib attachments and at any access cut-out.

- Ribs: inspect rib caps and webs for corrosion, especially at the lower-rib-to-lower-skin attachment where condensate accumulates.

- Stringers: inspect every stringer attachment to the lower skin and to the ribs.

- Sealant: inspect every fillet seal for cohesion failure (sealant pulling away from the substrate).

- Wiring and bonding: inspect every electrical bond, wire bundle, and fuel-quantity probe attachment.

## **4.2 Skin Damage Repair**

Wing skin repair is constrained by two competing requirements: the repair must restore the strength and fatigue life of the panel, and the repair must not produce a sealing failure that creates a fuel leak. Repairs that intrude into the tank space therefore use wet-install sealing throughout.

### **4.2.1 Lower-Surface Skin Repair**

Lower skin is fabricated from 2024-T351 alloy. The thickness varies from approximately 0.080 in. inboard to 0.040 in. outboard. The skin is highly fatigue-loaded; the SRM imposes tight repair limits, particularly on the inboard high-stress region. The procedure below is summarized for a Category-C repair using an external doubler.

**1.	**Confirm that the damage is within the negative-tolerance limit for the upper-doubler / lower-doubler combination defined by the SRM.

**2.	**Defuel the affected tank to below the lowest level of the work, and isolate the tank per AMM 28-11-00.

**3.	**Open the nearest internal access panel for support and inspection.

**4.	**Trim damage out of the skin in a smoothly contoured cut-out, rounded corners ≥ 25 mm (1.0 in.) radius.

**5.	**Fabricate the external (upper) doubler from 2024-T3 0.071 in. — one gauge thicker than the parent skin — to compensate for the holes drilled in the parent skin.

**6.	**Fabricate the internal doubler from 2024-T3 0.071 in. to a footprint that extends at least 50 mm (2.0 in.) past the external doubler on every side.

**7.	**Lay out the fastener pattern. The pattern is dense around the cut-out and reduces in density toward the doubler edge to taper the load into the parent structure.

**8.	**Drill, deburr, and countersink as described in Section 3.3.

**9.	**Apply wet-install sealant to all fastener holes and to all faying surfaces. Wet-install means the fastener is dipped in fresh sealant or the hole is filled with sealant immediately before installation.

**10.	**Install Hi-Lok HL18-6 fasteners in every hole. Use a barrier sealant under the collar where the collar is on the tank side.

**11.	**Tool a fillet seal of PR-1422 B-2 around the entire perimeter of both the external and internal doublers.

**12.	**Allow the sealant to fully cure (72 hours at 25°C nominal).

**13.	**Pressure-test the tank to verify no fuel-leak past the repair. The test pressure and acceptance criteria are given in AMM 28-11-04.

**14.	**Restore the tank corrosion protection and re-install the access panel.

**15.	**Refuel the tank, perform a static fuel-leak check, then a one-cycle pressurized leak check on the next flight.

| **WARNING** Any fuel-leak repair that has not been pressure-tested to the acceptance criterion must not be returned to service. A small leak past a wet-installed fastener can develop into a major leak under flight loads. |
| --- |

## **4.3 Spar Repair**

Spar repair is restricted by the SRM to a small set of defined cases. Most spar damage requires engineering disposition because the spar is the primary load-carrying member of the wing. The repair classes commonly handled at base maintenance are limited to web blends, isolated stiffener splices, and certain attachment-hole oversizing repairs. Cap repairs almost always require engineering.

### **4.3.1 Spar Web Blend-Out**

**1.	**Define the damage. Photograph, measure, and record per the inspection record practice.

**2.	**Confirm that the damage is in the web and not the cap radius. Cap-radius damage is not blend-able and goes to engineering.

**3.	**Mark the boundary of the blend. The blend boundary should be a smooth ellipse, not a rectangle. The blend depth should be tapered, with the deepest point at the center of the damage.

**4.	**Blend the damage out using progressively finer abrasive: 80 grit, 180 grit, 320 grit, 600 grit. The final finish should be no rougher than 32 microinches RMS.

**5.	**Measure the maximum remaining web thickness reduction. Compare against the SRM limits.

**6.	**Perform an eddy-current inspection of the blended area to verify no sub-surface crack remained after blend. Use a 200 kHz absolute probe, calibrated on the operator's reference standard for 7075-T6 sheet.

**7.	**Re-apply primer and corrosion protection.

| **CAUTION** Blend-out is not a structural repair in the load-carrying sense — it does not add material — but a damage-removal action. The remaining material must satisfy the SRM negative tolerance; if it does not, blend-out is not a valid disposition for the damage. |
| --- |

## **4.4 Slat and Flap Track Repair**

Slat and flap tracks are made from heat-treated and shot-peened steel forgings (typically AISI 4340 or 300M, heat-treated to approximately 1900 MPa ultimate tensile strength). Their inspection emphasizes the operating-roller contact area, the track-lug area, and the threaded fittings for the actuator attachments.

### **4.4.1 Slat Track Inspection**

**1.	**Extend the slats fully (typically to slat position 25 for inspection).

**2.	**Inspect every track for the following findings:

**a.	**Wear groove on the roller contact surface: a groove deeper than 0.005 in. is reportable; deeper than 0.015 in. requires track replacement.

**b.	**Scoring or galling on the roller contact surface.

**c.	**Cracks at the rear track fitting — perform a magnetic-particle inspection if any visual indication is found.

**d.	**Corrosion at the unprotected track ends.

**3.	**Inspect the rollers themselves for spalling, flat spots, and seizure. A roller that does not turn freely is rejected.

### **4.4.2 Replacement of a Slat Track**

| **WARNING** The leading-edge slat is a high-energy moving surface. Before any work on a slat or slat-track, the slat must be mechanically locked in the fully-extended position with the slat-down locks, and the slat hydraulic system must be depressurized and tagged in accordance with LOTO Section 1.4. |
| --- |

**1.	**Open the slat-track downstop bay covers.

**2.	**Index the track to the slat — mark the relative position so that re-installation maintains rigging.

**3.	**Remove the operating rollers and the rear track fittings per AMM 27-81-04 (synthetic reference).

**4.	**Remove the track from the slat by removing the four attachment bolts that secure the track-to-slat fitting.

**5.	**Install the new track in reverse order. Torque the four attachment bolts in two stages (60 % then 100 %) to the value specified in Appendix A.

**6.	**Verify slat rigging through a full extend / retract cycle on hydraulic ground power. Confirm slat position indication on the flight-deck display matches the slat position on the wing.

**7.	**Lubricate the rollers and the track per AMM 12-21-15.

**8.	**Return the slat to retracted position, remove the slat-down lock, and close all covers.

# **Chapter 5 — Landing Gear (ATA 32)**

ATA Chapter 32 covers the landing gear: main landing gear (MLG), nose landing gear (NLG), brakes and braking, tires and wheels, and the extension/retraction system. The 737-800 main gear is a single-axle, two-wheel arrangement; the nose gear is a single-axle two-wheel steerable assembly. All landing gear shock struts are oleopneumatic, charged with dry nitrogen and a hydraulic oil column.

## **5.1 Main Landing Gear Inspection**

**5.1 Reference Information**

| **Item** | **Detail** |
| --- | --- |
| Effectivity | All 737-800 |
| Reason for the Job | Daily and weekly walkaround inspection of MLG |
| Personnel Required | One technician (daily); two technicians (weekly with retraction test) |
| Elapsed Time | Daily 30 min; weekly 4 h |

### **5.1.1 Daily Inspection Items**

**1.	**Inspect the strut chrome plating for damage. The chrome surface is the seal running surface and any pitting, scratching, or corrosion compromises the seal.

**a.	**Acceptable: smooth, clean, unblemished chrome surface.

**b.	**Rejectable: any chrome pitting; any longitudinal scratch exceeding 0.005 in. depth; any rust spot; any chrome flaking.

**2.	**Measure the strut extension. With the aircraft at empty weight on the ramp, the exposed chrome length above the upper seal gland should be within the limits printed on the strut placard. Typical limits for the 737-800 MLG are 7.0 to 9.5 in., temperature-corrected.

**3.	**Inspect every visible hose, line, and fitting for leakage. A small wet patch is reported and monitored; a continuous drip or active stream requires immediate corrective action.

**4.	**Inspect the tire and wheel: tread depth, sidewall condition, valve cap presence, hub-bolt torque-seal stripe alignment.

**5.	**Inspect the brake assembly: wear-pin extension, line attachment torque-seal.

**6.	**Inspect the down-lock and the door uplock assemblies.

### **5.1.2 Weekly Inspection Additions**

In addition to the daily items, the weekly inspection adds:

- Retract / extend test on hydraulic ground power, with the aircraft on jacks. Verify smooth extension and retraction within the specified time bands.

- Door clearance check during retraction — typically 6 mm (0.25 in.) minimum at the closest point.

- Visual inspection of the trunnion bushings and the upper-arm pivot points for play.

- Function test of the down-lock release.

- Service of the shock-strut nitrogen pressure if the daily extension reading is at the low end of the range.

| **WARNING** Landing gear retraction must be performed only with the aircraft properly jacked, ground-locks removed, hydraulic system pressurized, and all personnel clear of the gear bay, wheel well, and door swing path. A retraction with a person in the wheel well is fatal. |
| --- |

### **5.1.3 Shock-Strut Servicing**

Servicing of the shock strut consists of two operations: replenishing the hydraulic fluid column, and recharging the nitrogen column to the correct pressure. The two operations are normally performed together, in the order fluid-first, nitrogen-second.

**1.	**Position the aircraft on level ground. Chock fore and aft.

**2.	**Deflate the strut by releasing nitrogen through the service valve, until the strut is fully compressed.

**3.	**Connect a clean transfer pump to the fluid service port.

**4.	**Pump approved hydraulic fluid (typically Skydrol LD-4 for the 737-800 — verify per the strut placard) into the strut until clean fluid free of bubbles flows from the bleed port.

**5.	**Close the bleed port, disconnect the pump, and cap the fluid service port.

**6.	**Connect a regulated nitrogen source to the gas service valve.

**7.	**Slowly admit nitrogen, observing the strut extension. Stop charging when the strut reaches the placard extension for the actual ramp weight and ambient temperature.

**8.	**Verify the charge pressure with a calibrated gauge.

**9.	**Cap the service valves and record the servicing in the aircraft technical log.

| **CAUTION** Use only dry nitrogen for shock-strut charging. Air or oxygen-contaminated nitrogen can support combustion in the presence of finely atomized hydraulic fluid — a phenomenon documented in landing-gear over-pressurization events. |
| --- |

## **5.2 Tire and Wheel Servicing**

The 737-800 main tires are typically a 44 × 17.5 R20 size, rated for the aircraft maximum landing weight at the operating speed. Tires are replaced at scheduled tread-wear limits or on damage, whichever comes first. Wheels are inspected and rotated through overhaul on the wheel-cycle program.

### **5.2.1 Tire Pressure Servicing**

**1.	**Allow the tire to cool for at least 2 hours after the last landing. Servicing a hot tire under-charges it (the pressure will drop as the tire cools).

**2.	**Confirm the placard pressure for the actual aircraft weight from the operator's tire-pressure chart.

**3.	**Remove the valve cap and connect the dry-nitrogen service line.

**4.	**Top up the tire to the placard pressure. Tires are normally serviced with dry nitrogen, not air; nitrogen avoids the combustion risk associated with overheated tires.

**5.	**Replace the valve cap.

**6.	**Record the servicing.

### **5.2.2 Tire Removal and Installation**

| **WARNING** A failed tire-wheel assembly can release stored gas pressure with explosive force. After removing the wheel from the aircraft, place the wheel in a tire cage with the valve stem pointing away from personnel before any disassembly is attempted. Reassembled wheels must be inflated in a cage. |
| --- |

**1.	**Lift the affected wheel clear of the ground using approved axle jacks. Place a maintenance stand under the gear leg for backup support.

**2.	**Remove the wheel-axle cotter pin, remove the axle nut, and slide the wheel from the axle.

**3.	**Place the wheel in the tire cage. Deflate the tire by removing the valve core; allow the gas to escape fully before any further work.

**4.	**Disassemble the wheel: remove the tie bolts (typical 24 to 32 fasteners depending on wheel design), separate the two halves of the wheel, and remove the tire.

**5.	**Inspect each wheel half for cracks (eddy-current inspection at the bead seat), for corrosion, and for thread damage at the tie-bolt holes.

**6.	**Re-assemble with the replacement tire. Use only the correct tie bolts; cross-thread or mixed-grade hardware compromises the wheel.

**7.	**Torque the tie bolts in the cross-pattern sequence to the placard torque, in three stages of 30%, 60%, and 100%.

**8.	**Inflate the tire to the placard pressure, in the tire cage. Verify no bead-seat leak with soap-water solution.

**9.	**Install the wheel on the axle. Torque the axle nut to the specified value and install the cotter pin.

| **NOTE** New tires require a 'cure period' of 12 hours at service pressure before first taxi. Skip-cure tires (factory pre-cured) are identified by a green marking on the sidewall. |
| --- |

## **5.3 Brake Assembly Replacement**

The 737-800 carbon brake heat-pack consists of stacked rotors and stators contained in a torque tube and pressure plate assembly. Brake replacement is driven by wear-pin extension reaching the minimum value, by an over-temperature event, or by scheduled replacement at the brake's design service limit.

### **5.3.1 Removal Procedure**

**1.	**Apply LOTO to the brake hydraulic isolation valve. The brake accumulator must be depressurized; verify zero pressure on the accumulator gauge.

**2.	**Remove the wheel per Section 5.2.

**3.	**Disconnect the brake hydraulic line at the brake. Cap immediately to prevent contamination.

**4.	**Disconnect the wear-pin sensor and any temperature-sensor harness from the brake.

**5.	**Remove the brake-to-axle attachment bolts (typical 6 or 8 bolts at the axle flange).

**6.	**Slide the brake assembly off the axle. A worn brake is heavy — approximately 70 kg; use a brake-handling dolly.

**7.	**Inspect the torque-tube splines on the axle for wear and corrosion. Report any finding to the duty engineer before installing a new brake.

### **5.3.2 Installation Procedure**

**1.	**Verify the new brake by part number, lot number, and shelf-life expiry on the carbon heat-pack.

**2.	**Slide the new brake onto the axle, aligning the torque-tube splines.

**3.	**Install and torque the axle-flange bolts per Appendix A.

**4.	**Connect the brake hydraulic line. Hand-start the fitting, then torque to the specified value.

**5.	**Connect the wear-pin and temperature-sensor harness.

**6.	**Install the wheel.

**7.	**Pressurize the brake hydraulic system and bleed the brake to remove all air. Bleeding is complete when fluid free of bubbles flows from the bleed port for at least 1 minute.

**8.	**Perform a brake function test: apply normal brake; verify pressure on the brake-pressure indicator; release; verify pressure decays to zero. Repeat using the alternate brake source.

**9.	**Confirm wear-pin extension reads 'full' on the new brake.

**10.	**Remove LOTO; return the aircraft to service.

| **CAUTION** New carbon brakes require a bedding-in cycle: typically five normal-energy brake applications during the first taxi after installation. Bedding-in establishes the transfer-film layer that gives carbon brakes their service-life friction characteristic. Do not perform a high-energy rejected-takeoff brake check on a brand-new brake without bedding-in. |
| --- |

# **Chapter 6 — Engine and Nacelle (ATA 71/72/54)**

The 737-800 is powered by two CFM International CFM56-7B series turbofan engines, each rated at approximately 121 kN (27,300 lbf) of static thrust depending on the specific dash number. The engine is mounted under the wing through a forward and aft mount that transmit thrust, torque, and inertial loads through the strut/pylon into the wing front spar.

This chapter covers external inspection, borescope inspection of the gas path, and inspection of the thrust-reverser system. Hot-section work, module exchange, and full engine removal are scoped to the engine OEM's CMM and to the operator's heavy-maintenance procedures and are referenced here for context only.

## **6.1 Engine Inspection (Walk-around)**

**6.1 Reference Information**

| **Item** | **Detail** |
| --- | --- |
| Effectivity | All 737-800 with CFM56-7B engines |
| Reason for the Job | Pre-flight, transit, and daily inspection of the propulsion installation |
| Personnel Required | One technician |
| Elapsed Time | 10 to 15 minutes per engine |

### **6.1.1 Inlet and Fan Inspection**

| **WARNING** Allow the engine fan to come to a full stop before approaching the inlet. A wind-milling fan can rotate fast enough to inflict serious injury and is invisible from outside the inlet. Verify by visual confirmation through the inlet that the fan is stationary. |
| --- |

**1.	**Inspect the inlet lip for damage. The inlet leading edge is a stress concentration that is vulnerable to bird strike, hail, and runway debris.

**a.	**Acceptable: superficial scuff or scratch within the inlet-lip allowable damage chart of the SRM.

**b.	**Reject (replace inlet): any crack; any dent deeper than 1.5 mm (0.060 in.) on the lip radius; any sharp gouge.

**2.	**Inspect the inlet cowl interior acoustic panels for damage. Acoustic-panel face-sheet damage exposing the honeycomb core is a finding requiring repair before next flight.

**3.	**Inspect every visible fan blade for damage. The fan blade is the most frequently damaged engine component.

| **Defect** | **Allowable** | **Reportable** | **Reject** |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Leading-edge nick | Smooth blend, depth ≤ 1.0 mm (0.040 in.) | Depth 1.0–2.5 mm; record and re-inspect at next opportunity | Depth > 2.5 mm; sharp profile; multiple blades affected |
| Leading-edge dent | ≤ 1.5 mm depth, smoothly contoured | 1.5–3.0 mm; in-service inspection plan | > 3.0 mm; angular profile |
| Tip curl | Smooth deflection ≤ 2.0 mm | 2.0–3.5 mm; engineering review | > 3.5 mm; cracked or torn tip |
| Crack | None permitted | Not applicable | Any crack of any length |
| Trailing-edge nick | Smoothly blended, ≤ 0.5 mm | 0.5–1.5 mm | > 1.5 mm; multi-blade trailing-edge damage |

**4.	**Inspect the fan spinner (nose cap) for damage and security. Verify any visible weep-hole is clear (typically aft of the spinner).

**5.	**Inspect the fan-blade root attachment for evidence of fretting (red iron-oxide dust) or sealant displacement.

**6.	**Through the fan, inspect the booster (low-pressure compressor) inlet guide vanes for damage and the outlet of the fan duct for foreign-object indications.

### **6.1.2 Cowl, Fan-Reverser, and Strut Inspection**

**1.	**Inspect the fan-reverser cowls for skin damage, hinge wear, and latch security. The 737-800 fan reverser is a cascade-type, fan-bypass-only design with split half-cowls.

**2.	**Inspect the area under the engine for fluid stains. Stain colour helps identify the source:

**a.	**Clear or yellow-brown stain: engine oil (typical from VBV or starter ports).

**b.	**Dark brown or black stain with carbon: combustion-section leakage.

**c.	**Bright red or purple stain: hydraulic fluid (Skydrol; engine-driven hydraulic pump or supply line).

**d.	**Clear fuel-scented stain: Jet A from fuel-system component leakage.

**3.	**Inspect the exhaust nozzle and exhaust plug for damage. Carbon deposit on the nozzle is normal; cracking or panel damage is not.

**4.	**Inspect the strut access doors and panels for security.

**5.	**Confirm the engine-installation drain mast is free of obstruction.

| **NOTE** If any fluid stain extends across more than 100 mm of nacelle skin or appears to be active (wet, freshly deposited), check the appropriate engine fluid level before flight and report the finding for follow-up. |
| --- |

## **6.2 Borescope Inspection**

Borescope inspection (BSI) is the principal method for monitoring the internal condition of the engine gas path without disassembly. The CFM56-7B has dedicated borescope access ports at every accessible stage of the high-pressure compressor, the combustor, and the high-pressure and low-pressure turbines. The detailed port locations and inspection criteria are defined in the engine OEM's BSI manual; this section addresses procedural and safety aspects relevant to performing a BSI.

**6.2 Reference Information**

| **Item** | **Detail** |
| --- | --- |
| Effectivity | CFM56-7B engines |
| Reason for the Job | Scheduled BSI per maintenance program; post-event inspection (e.g., reported FOD, EGT exceedance, vibration) |
| Personnel Required | BSI-trained technician + assistant |
| Elapsed Time | 4–8 h for a routine BSI; up to 16 h for an extended inspection |

### **6.2.1 Pre-BSI Conditions**

**1.	**Engine must be cool. The minimum allowed core temperature for BSI is given in the engine manual; for the CFM56-7B it is typically around 50°C.

**2.	**Apply LOTO to the engine start system, the engine ignition system, and the engine fuel-shutoff valve.

**3.	**Position the borescope cart with workspace for the borescope display, controls, and recorded video.

**4.	**Verify the borescope probe is the correct length and diameter for the port. Using a probe that is too long can damage the engine on insertion; one too short cannot reach the inspection feature.

| **WARNING** Borescope insertion past the seal on a port can introduce ambient contamination into the engine. Wipe every probe with lint-free cloth before insertion. Never use a probe that has been dropped or has visible damage to the tip. |
| --- |

### **6.2.2 Manual Engine Rotation**

During the BSI, the engine must be rotated by hand or by a powered rotation tool ('motoring' the engine without starter ignition is normally avoided for routine BSI to preserve the LOTO condition). Manual rotation is achieved through the accessory gearbox starter pad, with the appropriate adapter installed.

**1.	**Install the manual rotation adapter on the engine accessory gearbox.

**2.	**Two technicians coordinate: one operates the rotation tool, one monitors the borescope view.

**3.	**Rotate at the gearbox speed defined in the manual (typically 1–4 rpm at the gearbox, which corresponds to a slow turn of the N2 core).

**4.	**Inspect each rotor stage as it passes the port: each blade leading and trailing edge, each visible face, and the airfoil tip.

### **6.2.3 Common BSI Findings**

- HP compressor: leading-edge erosion, tip rub, foreign-object impact damage on first stages.

- Combustor: liner cracks, hot streaks, fuel-nozzle carbon.

- HP turbine: nozzle-guide vane (NGV) bow trailing-edge cracks, blade tip burn, thermal-barrier-coating (TBC) loss.

- LP turbine: blade tip wear, shroud rubs.

| **NOTE** Record every finding with high-resolution still images and a short video clip during slow rotation. The BSI record is part of the engine continued-airworthiness file. |
| --- |

## **6.3 Thrust Reverser**

The CFM56-7B fan thrust reverser is a translating-cowl, cascade-type, fan-bypass-only design. On selection, the upper and lower halves of the fan reverser cowl translate aft, exposing radial cascade vanes and deploying blocker doors into the fan bypass duct. The system redirects approximately 50% of the fan thrust forward, contributing to deceleration on landing rollout.

**6.3 Reference Information**

| **Item** | **Detail** |
| --- | --- |
| Effectivity | All 737-800 |
| Reason for the Job | Scheduled inspection and functional test of the thrust reverser |
| Personnel Required | Two technicians |
| Elapsed Time | 6 h per engine |

### **6.3.1 Thrust-Reverser Inspection**

**1.	**Confirm the thrust reverser is in the stowed position before opening the cowl. Apply mechanical down-locks if the cowl is opened with the reverser unstowed for any reason.

**2.	**Open both fan-cowl halves on the affected engine. Engage the cowl hold-open rods.

**3.	**Inspect each cascade vane for damage, missing vanes, or contamination by sealant or paint.

**4.	**Inspect each blocker door for surface damage, hinge condition, and linkage integrity.

**5.	**Inspect the translating cowl track wear-strips for wear, damage, and lubrication condition.

**6.	**Inspect the hydraulic actuator rod, end fitting, and rod-seal area for leakage.

**7.	**Inspect the auto-restow valve, isolation valve, and directional control valve assemblies for security and leakage.

### **6.3.2 Functional Test**

| **WARNING** During a reverser functional test, the cowl translates aft at high speed. Ensure no personnel are aft of the engine within the cowl swing arc, and that all temporary tools, cowl rods, and ground-support equipment are clear before pressurizing the system. |
| --- |

**1.	**With the affected engine off and the cowl latches closed, apply hydraulic ground power.

**2.	**From the flight deck, position the reverser-test selector or arm the test condition per the operator's procedure.

**3.	**Command 'deploy'. Verify reverser deploys fully within the specified time (typically less than 2 seconds for full travel).

**4.	**Verify the reverser deploy indication on the flight-deck synoptic and on the engine indicating panel.

**5.	**Command 'stow'. Verify reverser stows fully within the specified time.

# **Chapter 7 — Flight Controls (ATA 27)**

ATA Chapter 27 covers the primary and secondary flight controls: ailerons, elevators, rudder, stabilizer trim, leading-edge slats, trailing-edge flaps, and spoilers. The 737-800 retains the conventional cable-and-pulley architecture for the primary controls, supplemented by hydraulic power for the rudder and the elevator at high airspeed. The flap and slat systems are fully hydraulic with mechanical backup. The spoiler system is fully hydraulic.

## **7.2 Elevator Service**

The 737-800 elevator is a two-section control surface — left and right — mounted on the trailing edge of the horizontal stabilizer. The elevators are linked to the column through a cable system and to the elevator-feel computer and to two parallel hydraulic Power Control Units (PCU). The system retains manual reversion through the cable system if both hydraulic systems are lost.

### **7.2.1 Daily Inspection**

**1.	**Inspect both elevator surfaces externally for skin damage, missing or loose static dischargers, and trim-tab linkage condition.

**2.	**Inspect the elevator-to-stabilizer gap at the leading edge; the gap should be even across the span.

**3.	**Cycle the elevators by hand (with hydraulic power off) to confirm freedom of motion and absence of binding.

**4.	**Cycle the elevator trim tab and verify smooth response.

### **7.2.2 PCU Removal and Installation**

The PCU is a tandem-piston hydraulic actuator that converts hydraulic pressure into elevator drive force. Each elevator has its own PCU; PCUs are typically replaced on a hard-time basis or on a confirmed fault.

| **WARNING** The elevator PCU is positioned in a confined area under the horizontal stabilizer. The aft fuselage maintenance position requires fall protection. The work area is also above an open vertical drop into the aft pressure bulkhead — secure all tools to the harness or to the structure with retention lanyards. |

**1.	**Apply LOTO to hydraulic Systems A and B.

**2.	**Open the access panels above and below the affected elevator inboard end.

**3.	**Lock the elevator in the neutral position using the maintenance lock pins.

**4.	**Disconnect the two hydraulic supply lines and the two return lines at the PCU. Cap and bag every fitting.

## **7.3 Rudder Service**

The 737-800 rudder is a single hydraulically powered control surface on the trailing edge of the vertical stabilizer. The rudder is driven by a Main Rudder PCU (powered by both hydraulic Systems A and B in active-active configuration with a single output ram) and a Standby Rudder PCU (powered by the Standby hydraulic system, providing rudder authority if Systems A and B are both lost).

### **7.3.1 Rudder Functional Test**

**1.	**Apply hydraulic ground power. Confirm pressure on Systems A, B, and Standby.

**2.	**From the flight deck, displace the rudder pedals fully left and fully right. Verify rudder travel matches the rigging marks at the rudder hinge fitting.

**3.	**Verify smooth motion without overshoot or hunting.

**4.	**Switch to System A only; repeat. Switch to System B only; repeat. Verify rudder responds normally in each single-system condition.

**5.	**Activate the standby rudder system; repeat. Verify rudder responds in standby with reduced rate.

**6.	**Verify the yaw-damper input by selecting yaw damper on; observe small commanded inputs from the yaw-damper through the rudder pedal feedback.

## **7.4 Spoilers**

The 737-800 has six flight spoilers and four ground spoilers per wing-set, hydraulically powered through individual spoiler actuators. # **Chapter 8 — Hydraulic Systems (ATA 29)**

The 737-800 hydraulic system provides power for primary and secondary flight controls, landing gear extension and retraction, wheel brakes, nose-wheel steering, thrust reversers, trailing-edge flap drive, and leading-edge slat drive. The aircraft uses three independent 3,000 psi (20,684 kPa) hydraulic systems: System A, System B, and Standby. System A and B are full-pressure systems served by engine-driven pumps (EDPs) and electric motor-driven pumps (EMDPs). The Standby system is an emergency system pressurized only when a primary system fails or during specific operational phases such as autoland.

All three systems use Skydrol LD-4 or compatible phosphate-ester hydraulic fluid (BMS 3-11 specification). The fluid is fire-resistant but chemically aggressive to skin, paint finishes, and many elastomers. Maintenance personnel must observe strict handling precautions and use only authorized seal and hose materials when servicing the system.

| **WARNING** Skydrol LD-4 hydraulic fluid is hazardous to skin and eyes and is a strong irritant. Wear chemical-resistant gloves (nitrile minimum), splash goggles, and a face shield when working with the fluid or opening pressurized lines. Flush any contact area with cool water for at least 15 minutes and seek medical attention. Skydrol is also extremely aggressive to paint and most non-fluorinated elastomers — wipe spills immediately and dispose of contaminated rags as regulated hazardous waste. |
| --- |

| **CAUTION** Never substitute mineral-based hydraulic fluid (MIL-H-5606 or equivalent red fluid) for Skydrol. The two fluids are chemically incompatible and seal degradation will occur within hours, causing system-wide leakage. If cross-contamination is suspected, drain the affected system completely, replace all seals and accumulator bladders, and flush per the operator's contamination-recovery procedure before returning the system to service. |
| --- |

## **8.1 System Architecture**

### **8.1.1 System A**

System A is pressurized in flight by the No. 1 engine-driven pump (EDP A) and on the ground or as a backup by EMDP A, which is powered from the AC ground service bus or main AC bus 1. System A powers the inboard flight spoilers, the alternate brake system, autopilot A servos, the power transfer unit (PTU), and contributes to flap and slat extension. System A reservoir is in the left main wheel well and is pressurized by 18th-stage bleed air through a reservoir pressurization module to suppress cavitation at the pump inlet.

### **8.1.2 System B**

System B is pressurized in flight by the No. 2 engine-driven pump (EDP B) and on the ground or as a backup by EMDP B, which is powered from main AC bus 2. System B powers the outboard flight spoilers, the normal brake system, autopilot B servos, nose-wheel steering, the ground spoilers, leading-edge slats (alternate path), and yaw damper. System B reservoir is in the right main wheel well and is pressurized in the same manner as System A.

### **8.1.3 Standby System**

The Standby system is a single-pump, single-reservoir, lower-flow system. It uses an AC motor-driven pump that is powered automatically when either the rudder, leading-edge slats and flaps, or thrust reversers lose primary hydraulic power, and may also be selected manually by the flight crew. The Standby system reservoir is a smaller bladder-type reservoir located in the right wheel well immediately aft of the System B reservoir.

### **8.1.4 Power Transfer Unit (PTU)**

The PTU is a hydraulically driven hydraulic pump that uses System A pressure to drive a System B pump element without transferring fluid between the systems. The PTU activates automatically when System B engine pump pressure is low and leading-edge devices or autoslat are commanded. The PTU output is limited to roughly 6 gpm (22.7 L/min), enough to drive leading-edge devices at reduced rate but not enough to support normal landing gear, brake, or flight-control demand.

## **8.4 Bleeding the Hydraulic System**

Air entrained in the hydraulic fluid causes spongy actuator response, accelerated cavitation damage to the pumps, and elevated reservoir foaming. Bleeding is required whenever a major component (pump, accumulator, large actuator, or any line carrying significant volume) is opened to atmosphere.

**1.	**Service the reservoir to the FULL band before bleeding. Air at the pump inlet will defeat all bleed effort downstream.

**2.	**Identify the highest point of the disturbed circuit. Air will collect there.

**3.	**Open the highest bleed port slightly. Bleed ports are typically located at actuator caps, accumulator manifolds, and at the top of major filter housings.

**4.	**Run the EMDP and cycle the affected user (slowly) until fluid expelled from the bleed port is free of bubbles for at least ten seconds.

**5.	**Close the bleed port and torque the bleed plug per the value shown adjacent to the port.

**6.	**Repeat for any remaining bleed points.

**7.	**Re-service the reservoir.

| **CAUTION** Do not run an EDP or EMDP dry. Cavitation damage to the pistons and barrel can occur in as little as 20 seconds of dry running. Always confirm reservoir level before applying motor power to a pump. |

## **8.6 Hydraulic Fluid Sampling and Analysis**

Fluid sampling provides an early warning of internal wear, water ingress, and chemical breakdown. The operator's fluid-analysis program normally specifies sampling at 1,000 flight-hour intervals or after a known contamination event.

### **8.6.1 Sampling Procedure**

**1.	**Operate the affected system for at least 5 minutes to warm and circulate the fluid.

**2.	**Identify the dedicated sampling port (usually a Snap-tite or equivalent quick-disconnect at the return manifold).

**3.	**Wipe the sampling port with a Skydrol-approved lint-free cloth.

**4.	**Connect the sampling probe and sample container. Use only containers supplied by the analysis laboratory; ordinary bottles will contaminate the sample.

**5.	**Bleed a small purge volume to clear the probe before capturing the analysis sample.

**6.	**Capture the analysis sample (typically 4 fl oz / 120 mL).

**7.	**Disconnect, cap the port, and label the sample with aircraft serial, system, date, flight hours, and sampler identification.

### **8.6.2 Interpretation of Results**

Analysis results are typically reported with particle counts at multiple size ranges (per ISO 4406 or equivalent), water content (Karl Fischer titration, ppm), acid number (TAN), and elemental wear concentrations (Fe, Cu, Al, Cr) via spectroscopy.

# **Chapter 9 — Electrical Systems (ATA 24)**

The 737-800 electrical system provides AC and DC power for all aircraft loads. The primary AC sources are two engine-driven integrated drive generators (IDGs), each rated at 90 kVA, 115 V, 400 Hz. Secondary AC is supplied by an auxiliary power unit (APU) generator (also 90 kVA), and by external ground power. DC power is derived from four transformer rectifier units (TRUs), each producing 28 V DC at 75 A nominal output, and is supported by two main aircraft batteries (24 V nominal nickel-cadmium, 48 A·h).

| **WARNING** All work on the electrical system requires application of LOTO to the relevant bus and verification of de-energization with a calibrated voltage probe at the point of work. Aircraft electrical buses can carry sufficient stored energy (in TRU capacitors, motor windings, and battery banks) to produce a hazardous shock even after the source has been removed. Always discharge to a known ground after de-energization and before commencing work. |
| --- |

| **CAUTION** Battery handling requires specific PPE and ventilation. The nickel-cadmium electrolyte (potassium hydroxide) is strongly alkaline. Wear chemical-resistant gloves, splash goggles, and a face shield. Battery shops shall have ready access to emergency eyewash. Never short-circuit battery terminals — even a low-voltage NiCd cell can deliver several hundred amps into a low-impedance fault and damage tools, the cell, and the operator. |
| --- |

## **9.1 System Architecture**

### **9.1.1 AC Generation**

Each IDG converts the variable engine accessory-pad speed to a constant 12,000 rpm through a hydraulic constant-speed differential, then drives a brushless three-phase 115/200 V, 400 Hz generator. The IDG is mounted on the accessory gearbox and is fully self-contained — drive disconnect, oil cooling, and electrical output are within a single LRU. The IDG drive can be disconnected from the flight deck by the IDG DISCONNECT switch; a disconnect is mechanically latching and can only be reset on the ground by maintenance.

The APU generator is mounted on the APU accessory pad. Because the APU runs at a controlled speed of approximately 49,300 rpm reduced through the APU gearbox to 12,000 rpm at the generator pad, no constant-speed drive is required.

### **9.1.2 AC Distribution**

AC power is distributed through two transfer buses (AC Transfer Bus 1 and 2) and two main AC buses. The Bus Power Control Unit (BPCU) manages source selection, paralleling, and isolation. Under normal in-flight conditions, IDG 1 powers Transfer Bus 1 and IDG 2 powers Transfer Bus 2. Should a source fail, the BPCU automatically reconfigures to provide power to the affected bus from an available source — the alternate IDG, the APU generator, or ground power — while honoring the lockouts that prevent paralleling of unsynchronized sources.

### **9.1.3 DC Generation and Distribution**

Four TRUs (TR1, TR2, TR3, and the standby TR) convert 115 V AC to 28 V DC. TR1 and TR2 normally power the DC Bus 1 and DC Bus 2 respectively. TR3 powers the DC standby bus and the battery charger. The two main batteries (the main battery and the auxiliary battery) provide power to the hot battery bus, battery bus, and standby DC bus during start and as a last-resort backup. Battery duration following a total AC failure is sized to support flight-critical loads for at least 60 minutes in the typical configuration; specific endurance depends on cell condition and load shedding.

## **9.2 Integrated Drive Generator (IDG) Servicing**

IDG servicing consists of oil level check, oil change, filter element replacement, and on-condition replacement of the full LRU. Oil level is checked through a sight-glass on the side of the IDG case after the engine has been shut down for at least 5 minutes but not more than 30 minutes, so the oil has settled but not yet cooled to the point of contraction errors.

**Reference Information — IDG Oil Servicing**

| **Item** | **Detail** |
| --- | --- |
| Component | Integrated Drive Generator |
| Approved Oil | BMS 3-24 Type II (synthetic turbine oil), 5 cSt grade |
| Oil Capacity | Approximately 2.6 US qt (2.5 L) |
| ATA Reference | 24-11-15 |
| Servicing Tools | IDG service cart, IDG service hose, calibrated torque wrench, fill cap O-ring spares |
| Approximate Time | 0.4 manhours |
| Personnel | One (1) certifying mechanic |

### **9.2.1 Oil Level Check**

**1.	**Shut down the engine and start a timer.

**2.	**After 5 minutes but before 30 minutes, open the fan and core cowls on the affected engine.

**3.	**Locate the IDG sight glass on the inboard side of the IDG case.

**4.	**Wipe the sight glass clean.

**5.	**Note the oil level. The normal range is between the two horizontal index marks. If below the lower mark, top up per 9.2.2. If above the upper mark, drain per 9.2.3.

**6.	**Close the cowls.

### **9.2.2 Oil Top-Up**

**1.	**Connect the IDG service hose between the cart oil port and the IDG fill port.

**2.	**Open the cart valve. Add oil in 0.1 qt increments, observing the sight glass.

**3.	**When the level reaches the upper index mark, close the cart valve.

**4.	**Disconnect the hose, install a new O-ring on the fill cap, and torque the fill cap to 25 ± 3 in-lbf.

**5.	**Wipe any spilled oil and close the cowl.

### **9.2.3 IDG Replacement**

IDG replacement is performed under the following conditions: catastrophic oil loss with the unit disconnected by the flight crew (mandatory replacement before next flight); two consecutive in-flight disconnects on the same unit without identified cause; or detection of metal contamination at the magnetic chip detector exceeding the specified limits.

| **WARNING** The IDG weighs approximately 100 lb (45 kg) and must be supported on a hoist or compatible engine accessory stand during removal. Personnel injury and engine accessory-gearbox damage have occurred when an IDG was allowed to drop while disengaging from the gearbox splines. |
| --- |

**1.	**Confirm the engine is cool. Apply LOTO at the engine start lever, the IDG generator field breaker, and the IDG control panel switch.

**2.	**Open the fan and core cowls and install hold-open rods.

**3.	**Place a drip pan beneath the IDG.

**4.	**Disconnect the generator output connector (typically a three-phase terminal block under a cover at the rear of the IDG).

**5.	**Disconnect the control connector and the temperature sensor connectors.

**6.	**Disconnect the oil supply and return hoses (where externally cooled) and cap the fittings.

**7.	**Disconnect the disconnect-solenoid electrical connector and the speed/temperature sensor harness.

**8.	**Attach the IDG hoist sling to the lift fittings provided on the IDG case.

**9.	**Take up the slack on the hoist.

**10.	**Remove the V-band clamp (or the four flange bolts, depending on configuration) securing the IDG to the gearbox pad. Retain hardware.

**11.	**Carefully withdraw the IDG straight off the gearbox studs. Avoid lateral movement that could damage the splines.

**12.	**Immediately install a dust cover on the gearbox pad.

**13.	**Install the replacement IDG in reverse sequence. Use a new gasket. Torque mounting hardware per Appendix A.

**14.	**Reconnect all electrical, oil, and sensor connections.

**15.	**Service the IDG with oil per 9.2.2.

**16.	**Remove LOTO. Perform the IDG run-up test per 9.2.4.

**17.	**Make all AML and component records entries.

### **9.2.4 IDG Run-Up Test**

**1.	**Start the affected engine and stabilize at idle.

**2.	**On the IDG control panel, close the GEN BREAKER for the affected IDG.

**3.	**Verify the AC frequency on the corresponding bus is 400 Hz (±4 Hz).

**4.	**Verify the AC line-to-neutral voltage is 115 V (±2 V) on each of three phases.

**5.	**Apply representative bus loads (galley, lights, hydraulic EMDP) and verify voltage and frequency remain in band.

**6.	**Verify the IDG oil temperature stabilizes below 155 °C (the typical caution threshold).

**7.	**Open the GEN BREAKER and shut down the engine.

**8.	**After cooldown, recheck the IDG oil level per 9.2.1 and top up as required.

## **9.3 Battery Servicing**

The main battery and the auxiliary battery are nickel-cadmium 20-cell batteries (24 V nominal). Each battery is removed from the aircraft on a calendar interval for shop servicing — typical practice is removal every 90–180 days for capacity testing, electrolyte adjustment, and cell-by-cell inspection. On-aircraft battery work is limited to removal/installation, connector inspection, and visual inspection.

### **9.3.1 On-Aircraft Battery Removal**

| **WARNING** Before disconnecting the battery, verify the battery switch is OFF and confirm that no GPU or APU power is being supplied. Disconnecting a battery under load can produce a high-energy arc at the connector that will burn the technician and damage the connector beyond repair. |
| --- |

**1.	**Verify all battery switches at the overhead panel are in the OFF position.

**2.	**Open the battery access (typically the electrical/electronics — E&E — bay below the forward cabin).

**3.	**Loosen and remove the battery connector quick-disconnect. Verify with a calibrated voltage probe that no voltage is present at the aircraft side of the connector before proceeding.

**4.	**Loosen the battery hold-down strap.

**5.	**Lift the battery clear of the rack. A battery weighs approximately 60 lb (27 kg). Use two technicians or a battery hoist.

**6.	**Inspect the battery case for swelling, electrolyte residue, or burn marks. If any are present, isolate the battery and route to the shop for special handling.

**7.	**Install the replacement battery in reverse sequence.

**8.	**Verify the battery connector engages fully and the latch is positively engaged.

**9.	**Apply battery power and verify the battery voltage on the flight deck indicator is at or above 24 V.

**10.	**Verify the BATTERY DISCHARGE annunciator is not illuminated.

### **9.3.2 Battery Capacity Test (shop)**

Shop capacity testing is the definitive condition assessment for an aircraft battery. The shop test, performed per the battery OEM CMM, applies a known discharge current at a controlled temperature and measures the time-to-cutoff voltage. The capacity (in A·h) is calculated and compared to the rated capacity. Cells that have lost capacity below the OEM acceptance threshold (typically 80 percent of rated) are replaced individually before the next service cycle.

## **9.4 Wiring and Connector Inspection**

Wiring discrepancies are a leading cause of intermittent faults in modern aircraft. The EWIS (Electrical Wiring Interconnection System) inspection regime requires routine general visual inspection (GVI) of accessible wiring and detailed inspection of harnesses subject to chafe, heat, or moisture. Inspection criteria below are summarized; the operator's EWIS program documents are the controlling reference.

### **9.4.1 General Visual Inspection (GVI)**

Inspect for the following discrepancies during any GVI of wiring or connectors:

- Damaged or missing harness clamps and tie-wraps.

- Wire insulation showing cracking, chafing, embrittlement, contamination, or evidence of heat damage.

- Connectors with bent or pushed-back pins.

- Connector backshells loose, missing strain relief, or with exposed strands.

- Evidence of fluid contamination — hydraulic, fuel, lavatory, or galley fluid intrusion into a harness or connector.

- Splice protection missing or migrated.

- Conduit cracks, missing grommets, or sharp edges adjacent to a wire bundle.

### **9.4.2 Connector Pin-to-Socket Inspection**

When an electrical fault is traced to a specific connector, perform a detailed pin-to-socket inspection:

**1.	**De-energize the circuit and apply LOTO.

**2.	**Disconnect the connector, taking care to align the keys properly and avoid bending pins.

**3.	**Inspect each pin for straightness, plating condition, and engagement marks.

**4.	**Inspect each socket for spring tension using an approved gauge pin. A socket that does not retain the gauge pin with the specified force is rejected.

**5.	**Inspect the connector shell sealing surface for corrosion or contamination.

**6.	**Inspect the EMI gasket (if fitted) for compression set or tearing.

**7.	**Clean as required with an approved electrical contact cleaner. Do not use a wire brush or abrasive on plated contacts.

**8.	**Reconnect, taking care to engage the locking ring fully.

**9.	**Perform a continuity check and an insulation-resistance check at the harness terminals before restoring power.

## **9.5 Circuit Breaker Maintenance**

Circuit breakers on the 737-800 are predominantly thermal-magnetic units mounted on the P6 and P18 panels in the flight deck. Maintenance is generally limited to inspection, replacement when failed, and exceptional functional testing using approved test equipment.

### **9.5.1 Inspection**

During flight-deck inspection, check the breakers for:

- Indicator collar visible (breaker tripped) versus flush (breaker set).

- Cracked or chipped phenolic body.

- Discoloration around the breaker collar (sign of heat distress).

- Loose mounting (gentle wiggle test, no movement permitted).

- Legible legend strip.

### **9.5.2 Breaker Replacement**

**1.	**Identify the breaker by panel coordinate and circuit name. Cross-check with the Wiring Diagram Manual (WDM).

**2.	**Apply LOTO to the upstream bus.

**3.	**From behind the panel, disconnect the wires from the breaker terminals. Tag each wire with its terminal designation to ensure correct reinstallation.

**4.	**Remove the breaker mounting nut and withdraw the breaker from the front of the panel.

**5.	**Install the replacement breaker. Confirm the rating (in amps) and the type (thermal vs. trip-free) match the IPL.

**6.	**Torque the mounting nut to 15 in-lbf (typical, refer to specific breaker manufacturer).

**7.	**Reconnect the wires per the tags. Torque terminal screws per the value on the breaker.

**8.	**Remove LOTO and restore the circuit. Perform a power-on functional check of the affected system.

| **NOTE** Repeated tripping of a breaker is a sign of a circuit fault, not a sign of a faulty breaker. Resist the temptation to replace a tripping breaker without first finding the underlying cause. Breakers are protective devices, and a repeatedly tripping breaker has very likely already done its job correctly. |

# **Chapter 10 — Avionics: Communications and Navigation (ATA 23/34)**

The 737-800 avionics suite consists of communication systems (ATA 23) and navigation systems (ATA 34). The architecture is built around a digital data bus (ARINC 429) backbone connecting the major LRUs. The Flight Management Computer (FMC), Display Electronics Units (DEUs), Mode Control Panel (MCP), Inertial Reference Systems (IRSs), Air Data Inertial Reference Units (ADIRUs in later builds), VHF/HF radios, transponders, ATC datalink, and the weather radar are the principal components.

This chapter covers the principal LRU replacement procedures, the on-aircraft tests required after replacement, the antenna inspection regime, and the database loading process. Avionics troubleshooting and shop repair are scoped to the operator's Fault Isolation Manual (FIM) and the OEM CMMs and are referenced here only where directly relevant to on-aircraft work.

| **WARNING** Many avionics LRUs are sensitive to electrostatic discharge (ESD). Always wear an ESD wrist strap connected to an approved ground point when handling avionics modules out of their packaging. Store LRUs in their approved ESD bags until ready to install. ESD damage is typically not immediately visible and may manifest as a latent failure weeks or months after the event. |
| --- |

| **CAUTION** Verify the FIN (Functional Item Number) and part number of any replacement LRU before installation. Many 737-800 avionics LRUs are visually identical between dash numbers but are functionally incompatible. An incorrect part number may result in an apparent successful install, but with system behavior anywhere from degraded to dangerously incorrect. |
| --- |

## **10.1 VHF Communication System**

The 737-800 is normally equipped with three VHF transceivers (VHF-1, VHF-2, VHF-3) providing voice communication in the 118–137 MHz aeronautical band. VHF-3 may be configured as the ACARS datalink radio, in which case it carries digital traffic in addition to or in place of voice. Each transceiver is mounted in the E&E bay and connects to an external antenna (typically a blade antenna on the upper or lower fuselage).

### **10.1.1 VHF Transceiver Replacement**

**Reference Information — VHF Transceiver Replacement**

| **Item** | **Detail** |
| --- | --- |
| Component | VHF Transceiver |
| Part Number (synthetic) | VHF-T8-001-XX (where XX identifies the customer dash number) |
| Quantity | 3 per aircraft |
| ATA Reference | 23-12-01 |
| Required Tools | ESD wrist strap, BITE handheld test set, calibrated VHF wattmeter, torque wrench (in-lbf) |
| Approximate Time | 0.7 manhours per unit |
| Personnel | One (1) certifying mechanic |

**1.	**Apply LOTO at the affected VHF circuit breaker on the P18 panel.

**2.	**Open the E&E bay and locate the transceiver in its rack.

**3.	**Don ESD wrist strap. Connect strap to ground.

**4.	**Loosen the front-mount thumb-wheels securing the unit in the rack.

**5.	**Withdraw the unit straight out — avoid rocking, which can damage the rear blind-mate connector.

**6.	**Place the removed unit in an ESD bag and label with aircraft serial, position, removal date, and reason.

**7.	**Inspect the rack rear connector for bent or recessed pins and clean if required.

**8.	**Inspect the rack cooling air port for blockage.

**9.	**Verify the part number of the replacement unit matches the IPL for this aircraft.

**10.	**Slide the replacement unit straight into the rack until the blind-mate connector seats.

**11.	**Tighten the thumb-wheels alternately to seat the unit fully.

**12.	**Remove LOTO and apply power.

**13.	**Perform the functional test per 10.1.2.

### **10.1.2 VHF Functional Test**

**1.	**Apply electrical power. Wait for the unit BITE self-test to complete (typically under 30 seconds).

**2.	**Verify no BITE faults are reported.

**3.	**Set the radio to a test frequency authorized by the airport authority — typically a maintenance test frequency or the ATIS broadcast for receive-only check.

**4.	**Receive check: confirm the audio is intelligible and squelch operates correctly.

**5.	**Transmit check (only on authorized frequency, with appropriate coordination): key the microphone and confirm forward power on the wattmeter is within the IPL-specified band (typically 25 W ±5 W nominal).

**6.	**Verify reverse power (reflected) is below the specified limit, indicating proper antenna and feed-line match.

**7.	**Make AML entry. Tag and route the removed unit per the operator's component-repair process.

## **10.2 Transponder and ADS-B**

The 737-800 is equipped with dual Mode-S transponders providing surveillance to ATC. With the ADS-B Out mandate, both transponders are normally configured to broadcast ADS-B Out using 1090ES (1090 MHz Extended Squitter) and to receive position from the GPS sensors. ADS-B In, where fitted, is supported by a separate receiver and display function.

### **10.2.1 Transponder Replacement**

**1.	**Apply LOTO to the affected transponder circuit breaker.

**2.	**Open the E&E bay and locate the transponder in its rack.

**3.	**Don ESD wrist strap.

**4.	**Loosen the thumb-wheels and withdraw the unit.

**5.	**Install the replacement unit, verifying part number and mod-level compatibility with the operator's installed configuration.

**6.	**Remove LOTO and apply power.

**7.	**Perform the transponder ramp test per 10.2.2.

### **10.2.2 Transponder Ramp Test**

The ramp test is performed using an FAA-approved (or local-authority-approved) IFR Mode-S/ADS-B test set.

**1.	**Position the test set such that the test antenna is in clear line-of-sight of the transponder antenna being tested (typically the lower antenna for ADS-B Out).

**2.	**Configure the test set for Mode-S interrogation.

**3.	**Interrogate the transponder and verify the unit responds with the correct Mode-S address.

**4.	**Verify the ADS-B Out broadcast contains: ICAO 24-bit address; flight ID; emitter category; latitude/longitude; altitude (barometric and geometric); velocity; navigation accuracy and integrity values.

**5.	**Verify altitude reporting agrees with the air data computer to within ±125 feet.

**6.	**Repeat for the second transponder using the appropriate antenna.

| **NOTE** ADS-B Out performance is monitored by air traffic control authorities. Aircraft that broadcast incorrect or stale position data may be issued a 'NUC=0' or similar advisory and required to address the issue before further IFR operation in the affected airspace. Always log ramp-test results in the maintenance records. |
| --- |

## **10.3 Inertial Reference System (IRS)**

The 737-800 IRS provides attitude, heading, position, and velocity reference for the flight management, autoflight, and display systems. The aircraft has two IRS LRUs (some configurations include three), each independent and providing redundant outputs. IRS alignment requires the aircraft to be stationary and to remain stationary throughout the alignment process; movement during alignment will produce position errors that cannot be corrected without restarting alignment.

### **10.3.1 IRS Replacement**

| **WARNING** Confirm the IRS mode-select switch is OFF before disconnecting power. Removing power from an aligned IRS will normally trigger an automatic shutdown sequence, but disconnecting under power can corrupt the unit's non-volatile memory and result in a unit that requires shop intervention to recover. |
| --- |

**1.	**Move the IRS mode-select switch on the flight deck to OFF for the affected IRS.

**2.	**Wait at least 30 seconds for the unit to perform its internal shutdown.

**3.	**Apply LOTO at the IRS circuit breakers.

**4.	**Open the E&E bay and locate the IRS in its rack.

**5.	**Don ESD wrist strap.

**6.	**Loosen the hold-down knobs and withdraw the unit. The IRS is heavier than a typical avionics LRU — approximately 25 lb (11.3 kg) — and should be supported with both hands.

**7.	**Place in an ESD bag. Tag with aircraft serial, position, removal date, and reason.

**8.	**Install the replacement unit. Verify part number and mod-level compatibility.

**9.	**Remove LOTO and apply power.

**10.	**Perform the IRS alignment per 10.3.2.

### **10.3.2 IRS Alignment**

**1.	**Confirm the aircraft is stationary, on a level surface, with parking brake set and chocks in place.

**2.	**Confirm no ground equipment is operating that produces aircraft motion (no engine runs, no jack-and-shake checks, no air-conditioning carts producing strong air flow against the airframe).

**3.	**Select the IRS mode switch to NAV.

**4.	**On the FMC IRS INIT page, enter the present-position latitude and longitude. The position must agree with the previous shutdown position within the limit specified by the unit (typically 1° of latitude).

**5.	**Monitor the alignment progress on the FMC. Full alignment requires approximately 10 minutes at mid-latitudes; longer at higher latitudes.

**6.	**On completion, verify the IRS reports alignment complete and that the residual ground velocity (a leakage figure) is below the specified limit. A high residual indicates either aircraft motion during alignment or a failed IRS.

**7.	**Cross-check the IRS-derived position against a known reference (the gate position from the airport survey or the GPS-derived position).

## **10.4 Flight Management Computer (FMC)**

The FMC integrates navigation, performance, and flight-plan data and provides the principal pilot interface for trip planning, vertical and lateral navigation guidance, and engine-out performance. The aircraft has two FMCs in a master/standby configuration.

### **10.4.1 Navigation Database Loading**

The FMC navigation database is updated every 28 days on the AIRAC cycle. Database loading is performed on the ground, with the aircraft powered, using an approved data loader (the portable data loader or the on-board airborne data loader, depending on the aircraft configuration).

**1.	**Verify the AIRAC effective date of the new database. The database must not be loaded before its effective date except in a 'pre-load' window approved by the operator.

**2.	**Apply power to the aircraft and bring up the FMC.

**3.	**On the FMC, navigate to the IDENT page and note the currently-active database AIRAC cycle and effective dates.

**4.	**Connect the data loader to the dataloader port (typically located on the P9 panel).

**5.	**Insert the database media. Initiate the load.

**6.	**Monitor the load. A full database load takes approximately 10–20 minutes depending on database size and loader speed.

**7.	**On completion, verify on the FMC IDENT page that the new AIRAC cycle is now installed.

**8.	**Verify the FMC does not report any database integrity faults.

**9.	**Make the appropriate AML entry showing AIRAC cycle, effective dates, and date of load.

| **NOTE** If the loader reports a checksum or integrity error during loading, do not attempt to use the partial database. Reload from a verified source or revert to the previous cycle. A corrupt navigation database can produce subtle but dangerous routing errors that may not be detected during preflight. |
| --- |

### **10.4.2 FMC LRU Replacement**

**1.	**Verify the affected FMC is the failed unit by checking the BITE log on the FMC CDU.

**2.	**Apply LOTO at the affected FMC circuit breaker.

**3.	**Open the E&E bay and locate the FMC in its rack.

**4.	**Don ESD wrist strap and withdraw the unit.

**5.	**Install the replacement unit, verifying part number and software revision.

**6.	**Remove LOTO and apply power.

**7.	**Load the current navigation database per 10.4.1.

**8.	**Load the aircraft performance database (operator-specific) if not pre-loaded in the LRU.

**9.	**Perform a functional check by entering a test flight plan and verifying lateral and vertical guidance computations.

## **10.5 Weather Radar**

The weather radar (typically a Honeywell RDR-4000 or similar in 737-800 builds) consists of a receiver/transmitter LRU in the E&E bay, a flat-plate antenna in the nose radome, and pilot-interface controls. The radar provides forward-looking weather detection, turbulence indication, and (in capable configurations) predictive windshear detection.

| **WARNING** Radar radiation hazard. The weather radar transmits high-power microwave energy. Personnel must not be in front of the antenna while it is radiating. Maintain a minimum distance of 20 feet (6 m) in the forward 60° arc from the nose during radar transmit operations. Fueling operations and operations near other aircraft also impose radar transmit prohibitions — refer to the operator's safety manual. |
| --- |

### **10.5.1 Antenna Removal**

**1.	**Apply LOTO at the weather radar circuit breaker. Place a placard at the radar control panel.

**2.	**Open the radome by releasing the radome latches and pivoting the radome to the open position. Support the radome with the prop rod.

**3.	**Don ESD wrist strap.

**4.	**Disconnect the antenna drive harness and the RF waveguide connection at the antenna.

**5.	**Remove the antenna mounting bolts and lift the antenna clear of the bulkhead. The antenna is fragile — handle by the perimeter only, never by the radiating surface.

**6.	**Cap the waveguide connection to prevent contamination.

### **10.5.2 Radome Inspection**

The radome is a composite structure transparent to microwave energy. Damage to the radome (paint loss, surface erosion, lightning damage, internal delamination) affects radar performance — typically degrading the antenna gain and increasing sidelobe levels. Inspect the radome with each access:

- Visual inspection of the outer surface for paint condition, erosion, and impact damage.

- Tap-test or instrument inspection of suspect areas for delamination.

- Inspection of the lightning-diverter strips for continuity, bonding, and erosion.

- Inspection of the radome hinge fittings and latches for cracks, looseness, and corrosion.

- Verification of the radome anti-static coating (where applicable) within continuity specification.

### **10.5.3 Lightning Diverter Strip Replacement**

Lightning diverter strips are conductive segmented strips bonded to the radome exterior that provide a controlled path for lightning attachment, preventing puncture of the composite radome wall. Strip erosion, joint corrosion, or bond failures require replacement.

**1.	**Identify the affected strip and verify the replacement part number from the IPL.

**2.	**Carefully scrape and remove the failed strip and the existing adhesive.

**3.	**Clean the bonding area with the approved cleaner (typically MEK or an approved equivalent), wiping with a single-direction stroke and a fresh wipe.

**4.	**Apply the approved adhesive (an electrically-conductive epoxy in many cases) per the SRM.

**5.	**Position the new strip and apply uniform pressure during cure.

**6.	**After cure, verify electrical continuity between the strip and the radome bond point.

**7.	**Apply protective overcoat per the SRM.

## **10.6 Pitot-Static System (ATA 34)**

The pitot-static system provides ram-air pressure (pitot) and static pressure references to the air data computers, standby instruments, and cabin pressure controller. The 737-800 has three pitot probes (Captain, First Officer, and Auxiliary) and multiple flush static ports paired on the left and right sides of the forward fuselage.

| **WARNING** Never apply ground-source pressure or suction to a pitot or static port without first ensuring all connected instruments are isolated or capable of withstanding the test pressures. Inadvertent over-pressurization or over-suction can damage altimeters, airspeed indicators, and the air data computer modules. Use only an approved pitot-static test set and verify all isolation valves are positioned correctly before applying test pressures. |
| --- |

### **10.6.1 Pitot Probe Removal**

**1.	**Confirm the pitot heater is OFF and has been off long enough to cool (probes can exceed 200 °C in operation).

**2.	**Apply LOTO to the pitot heat circuit breaker.

**3.	**Open the access panel behind the probe. Disconnect the heater electrical connector.

**4.	**Disconnect the pneumatic line at the rear of the probe. Cap the line and the probe port immediately.

**5.	**Remove the probe mounting screws. Withdraw the probe through the fuselage skin opening.

**6.	**Inspect the fuselage skin opening for damage and the probe seal for condition.

### **10.6.2 Pitot-Static System Leak Check**

After any work that disturbs a pitot or static line, a leak check is required. The leak check verifies the system holds a known pressure or vacuum for a specified time within an acceptable decay rate.

**1.	**Connect the approved test set to the appropriate probes and ports, using the manufacturer-supplied adapters.

**2.	**Verify all isolation valves are in the correct position to include the test ports and exclude the air data computers (if so instructed by the test set procedure).

**3.	**Apply a pressure (pitot) or suction (static) equivalent to a known calibrated altitude or airspeed — typical leak-check values are 1,000 feet altitude (static) and 100 knots airspeed (pitot).

**4.	**Allow the system to stabilize.

**5.	**Observe the decay over one minute. The decay must not exceed the allowed value specified in 34-11-00 (typically 100 ft/min for the static system and 10 kt/min for the pitot system).

**6.	**Vent the system slowly back to ambient. Never vent a pressurized or evacuated pitot-static system rapidly — connected instruments may be damaged.

**7.	**Disconnect the test set, restore the system to its normal configuration, and verify all isolation valves are back in their operating positions.

| **NOTE** A failed leak check requires systematic isolation of the leak. Common leak locations are at probe-to-fuselage seals, at line couplings, at instrument port fittings, and at drain valves that have stuck partially open. Use the soap-bubble or smoke-tracer techniques described in 34-11-00 to localize the leak before replacing components. |
| --- |

## **10.7 GPS System**

The 737-800 GPS configuration typically uses two multi-mode receivers (MMRs) that include GPS, VHF navigation (VOR/LOC/GS), and MLS capabilities. The MMRs provide position to the IRS/FMC for GPS-aided position keeping, ADS-B Out, and required-navigation-performance (RNP) operations. GPS antennas are flush-mounted on the upper fuselage forward of the wing root.

### **10.7.1 GPS Antenna Inspection**

- Inspect the antenna upper surface for sealant cracking, paint loss, and physical damage from hail or impact.

- Inspect the perimeter sealant for cracks, lifting, or contamination.

- Verify the antenna mounting fasteners are present, properly torqued, and not corroded.

- Inspect the coaxial connection at the rear of the antenna for moisture intrusion (look for white salt deposit at the connector backshell).

- Verify the antenna ground bond resistance is within 0.0025 ohms (typical) by approved bonding meter.

### **10.7.2 MMR Replacement**

**1.	**Apply LOTO at the affected MMR circuit breaker.

**2.	**Open the E&E bay and locate the MMR in its rack.

**3.	**Don ESD wrist strap. Withdraw the unit.

**4.	**Install the replacement unit, verifying part number, mod level, and software revision.

**5.	**Remove LOTO and apply power.

**6.	**Allow the unit to acquire GPS satellites — typical time to first fix is under 5 minutes if the unit has valid almanac data, or up to 15 minutes for a cold start.

**7.	**Verify the unit reports valid GPS position on the FMC POS REF page. Verify the GPS-derived position agrees with the IRS-derived position within the specified threshold.

**8.	**Verify the VOR/LOC/GS functions by tuning a known facility within range and confirming valid bearing and deviation indications.

| **NOTE** GPS performance is monitored continuously by the receiver autonomous integrity monitoring (RAIM) function. RAIM availability for a planned RNP procedure must be checked prior to dispatch on routes that depend on GPS navigation accuracy. The operator's flight-planning system normally performs this check, but maintenance should be aware that a GPS-related squawk may have RAIM implications. |
| --- |

## **10.8 Audio and Cockpit Voice Recorder Considerations**

The cockpit voice recorder (CVR) and the flight data recorder (FDR) are part of the avionics suite for regulatory purposes. Both are sealed LRUs and on-aircraft maintenance is limited to removal and replacement; data extraction and analysis are performed by approved facilities only.

### **10.8.1 CVR/FDR Removal**

**1.	**Confirm authorization for removal. CVR/FDR removal and any data download are tightly controlled procedures.

**2.	**Apply LOTO to the affected unit's circuit breaker.

**3.	**Access the recorder bay (typically in the aft fuselage).

**4.	**Don ESD wrist strap.

**5.	**Disconnect the unit's electrical connector and the underwater locator beacon (ULB) test connector, if installed separately.

**6.	**Remove the unit's hold-down hardware and withdraw the unit.

**7.	**Tag the unit with full traceability data — aircraft serial, position, removal date, removal reason, and any incident reference.

**8.	**Place the unit in an approved container for shipment if it is being routed for data download in connection with an event.

### **10.8.2 Underwater Locator Beacon (ULB) Battery**

The ULB battery has a service life — typically 6 years — after which the ULB must be replaced or its battery exchanged at an authorized facility. The battery service date is recorded on the unit's external label. The operator's maintenance program tracks this date and triggers replacement before expiry. An expired ULB battery is a regulatory dispatch issue.

# **Chapter 11 — Cabin and Environmental Systems (ATA 21)**

The 737-800 environmental control system (ECS) provides cabin pressurization, temperature control, ventilation, and air conditioning for the flight deck, passenger cabin, cargo holds, and avionics equipment cooling. The system uses engine bleed air (or APU bleed air on the ground) as its primary source of conditioned air. Two air conditioning packs (Pack 1 and Pack 2) located beneath the wing-body fairing process the bleed air through air-cycle machines to provide cooled, conditioned air to the distribution system.

Cabin pressurization is controlled by the digital cabin pressure controller (CPC) which modulates the outflow valve on the aft fuselage. The system maintains a maximum differential pressure of approximately 8.35 psi (57.6 kPa), which permits a sea-level cabin altitude up to a flight altitude of approximately 23,000 feet and an 8,000-foot cabin altitude at the certified maximum flight altitude of 41,000 feet.

| **WARNING** Bleed air ducting carries air at temperatures up to 450 °C (842 °F) and pressures up to 45 psig during operation. Allow the system to cool for at least 30 minutes after engine or APU shutdown before working on or near the bleed air ducts. Use temperature-rated gloves and confirm cool-to-touch with appropriate instruments before grasping any duct or fitting. |
| --- |

## **11.1 Air Conditioning Pack Servicing**

### **11.1.1 Pack Description**

Each pack consists of a primary heat exchanger, a secondary heat exchanger, an air-cycle machine (ACM) comprising a turbine, fan, and compressor on a common shaft, a water separator, and a temperature control valve. Bleed air enters the pack at high temperature and pressure, is cooled by ram air through the heat exchangers, expanded through the ACM turbine to extract energy and further cool the air, and water is condensed out by the separator before delivery to the mix manifold.

### **11.1.2 Pre-Flight Pack Operational Check**

**1.	**With APU running and bleed air available, select Pack 1 on the overhead panel.

**2.	**Verify Pack 1 outlet temperature initially rises (warm-up), then stabilizes within the operational range as the ACM spools up.

**3.	**Verify the pack outlet temperature indication agrees with the selected temperature on the cabin temperature controller.

**4.	**Listen for unusual mechanical noise from the pack bay. ACM bearing distress typically presents as a rising whine or a periodic chirping sound.

**5.	**Select Pack 2 and repeat steps 2 through 4.

**6.	**Operate both packs simultaneously and verify the mix manifold temperature is within control range.

## **11.2 Cabin Pressurization**

### **11.2.1 Outflow Valve Operational Check**

**1.	**On the ground with all doors closed, select the cabin pressure controller to MANUAL mode.

**2.	**Cycle the outflow valve in manual control from FULL CLOSED to FULL OPEN. Verify the valve travels smoothly and the position indicator agrees with the selection.

**3.	**Listen for unusual mechanical sounds — grinding, ticking, or hesitation in travel — that would indicate actuator or linkage distress.

**4.	**Return the controller to AUTO mode. Verify the valve modulates to the scheduled position for ground operation (typically full open or near full open).

### **11.2.2 Cabin Pressurization Leak Check**

A complete cabin pressurization leak check is performed after major work on the pressure vessel (door reseal, window replacement, repair to the pressurized skin, or any work that disturbs a pressure seal). The check uses ground bleed air supplied to the cabin while the outflow valve is closed, measuring the air supply rate required to maintain a target differential pressure.

| **WARNING** Cabin pressurization leak checks involve pressurizing the cabin to a substantial differential pressure with a ground supply. All persons inside the cabin during the check must be briefed and aware of the test. All doors must be confirmed locked. Pressure must be relieved by controlled venting of the outflow valve before any door is opened. A pressurized cabin door cannot be opened safely and attempting to do so may cause the door to fail open with explosive force. |
| --- |

**1.	**Brief all personnel involved. Post placards at each cabin door and the flight deck.

**2.	**Verify all doors, windows, hatches, and panels are properly closed and latched.

**3.	**Connect ground bleed-air supply to the appropriate test port.

**4.	**Configure the cabin pressure controller to the test mode per AMM 21-31-00.

**5.	**Pressurize the cabin slowly. Target differential pressure for the standard leak check is 4.5 psi (31 kPa) — never exceed the limit specified for the test.

**6.	**Allow the system to stabilize for at least 5 minutes.

**7.	**Record the supply flow required to maintain stable differential pressure. Compare to the limit specified in AMM 21-31-00. A flow rate exceeding the limit indicates the leakage budget is exceeded.

**8.	**If leakage is excessive, depressurize and use ultrasonic leak detection or soap-bubble methods at suspect locations — door seals, window seals, drains, plumbing penetrations — to localize the leak.

**9.	**Depressurize through the outflow valve. Verify the differential pressure is zero before unlatching any door.

# **Chapter 12 — Doors and Windows (ATA 52 / 56)**

This chapter covers maintenance of passenger doors, service doors, cargo doors, and the flight deck and cabin windows. The doors are plug-type and rely on cabin pressure to assist sealing — the door geometry is such that internal pressure presses the door against its sealing frame. Maintenance focus is on the sealing surfaces, the door operating mechanism, the latching system, and the emergency egress provisions.

## **12.1 Passenger Door Inspection**

The 737-800 has two forward passenger doors (1L, 1R) and two aft passenger doors (2L, 2R), each equipped with an emergency evacuation slide. Doors 1L and 2L are the primary boarding doors; doors 1R and 2R are typically service doors but are full-function emergency exits. Two overwing emergency exits (Type III) are located on each side at mid-cabin.

### **12.1.1 Daily / Pre-Flight Inspection**

- Inspect the door exterior for skin damage, dent, scratch, and corrosion within the inspection limits.

- Inspect the door seal for cuts, tears, set, and contamination. Heavy lubricants, paint, or dirt on the seal can break the seal and cause cabin air leak.

- Inspect the door handle and the emergency operation lever for free, complete travel.

- Inspect the slide bottle pressure gauge through the inspection window. Pressure must be within the green band — outside-band readings require slide servicing.

- Verify the slide arming lever (girt bar) is in DISARMED position when the door is not being prepared for flight.

### **12.1.2 Door Seal Replacement**

**1.	**Position the door in the FULL OPEN, latched position.

**2.	**Identify the seal segments by reference to the IPL. The seal is segmented; partial replacement is acceptable when only a portion is damaged.

**3.	**Remove the affected seal segment by releasing the seal-retaining strip and carefully peeling the seal from the channel.

**4.	**Clean the seal channel thoroughly. Solvent residue or adhesive residue from the previous installation will compromise the new seal.

**5.	**Install the new seal segment, pressing it firmly into the channel and confirming uniform seating.

**6.	**Apply a thin film of the approved seal lubricant (typically a silicone grease per BMS 3-31 or equivalent) on the sealing face. Do not over-apply — excess lubricant attracts dirt.

**7.	**Cycle the door several times to verify smooth operation.

**8.	**Perform a cabin leak check per 11.2.2 to verify seal performance under pressure.

## **12.2 Flight Deck Window Replacement**

The flight deck windows are multi-pane laminated assemblies with electrical heating elements for anti-icing and anti-fogging. The forward windshield panes (Captain and First Officer No. 1 windows) are bird-strike rated and contain structural elements that contribute to the pressure vessel. The side windows (No. 2 and No. 3) are smaller and not bird-strike rated.

| **WARNING** Flight deck window assemblies are heavy (the No. 1 window weighs approximately 26 lb / 12 kg) and must be supported during removal. The window glass is laminated with structural plies; a dropped or impacted window may have invisible internal damage that compromises the bird-strike capability. Treat removed windows with care and inspect thoroughly before any reinstallation. |
| --- |

### **12.2.1 No. 1 Window Removal**

**1.	**Apply LOTO to the window heater circuit breaker.

**2.	**Verify the window heater is cool — recently energized window heat can leave the window assembly at temperatures sufficient to cause burns or thermal shock from contact with cool tools.

**3.	**From inside the flight deck, remove the interior window trim panel.

**4.	**Disconnect the window heater electrical harness at the connector. Tag the connector for later identification.

**5.	**Remove the inner window retainer hardware. Retain in a labeled parts bag and note the orientation of any tapered or directional washers.

**6.	**Have one technician support the window from outside the aircraft using approved suction-cup lifting tools. Confirm the suction cups are securely attached and the lifting equipment is rated for the window weight.

**7.	**Remove the outer window retainer hardware.

**8.	**Carefully lift the window straight out of the frame opening. Avoid scraping the seal or sliding the window against the frame.

**9.	**Place the window in an approved support cradle. Never set a window flat on its face on the floor.

### **12.2.2 No. 1 Window Installation**

**1.	**Inspect the frame opening, the seal groove, the retainer channels, and all fastener holes. Look for corrosion, sealant residue, and elongated holes.

**2.	**Apply the approved frame sealant (PR-1422 B-2 or equivalent BMS 5-95 sealant) to the frame sealing surface in a uniform bead.

**3.	**Carefully position the new window into the opening with the lifting equipment.

**4.	**Install the outer retainer hardware finger-tight first to ensure correct alignment.

**5.	**Install the inner retainer hardware finger-tight.

**6.	**Torque all retainer fasteners per Appendix A in two passes, working in a star pattern from the center outward.

**7.	**Reconnect the heater harness. Verify the connector is fully seated and locked.

**8.	**Tool the sealant bead at the perimeter to a smooth, continuous fillet.

**9.	**Remove LOTO.

**10.	**Perform the window heater functional check per 12.2.3.

**11.	**Perform a cabin leak check after sealant cure time has elapsed (typically 72 hours minimum at 21 °C / 70 °F).

### **12.2.3 Window Heater Functional Check**

**1.	**Apply electrical power. Set the window heat switch to LOW.

**2.	**Verify the window temperature rises smoothly. Touch the inside surface periodically (with caution) to confirm warming.

**3.	**Select HIGH. Verify the window temperature increase is proportional and that the controller modulates without runaway heating.

**4.	**Use an infrared thermometer to spot-check the surface temperature distribution. Hot spots (temperature deltas exceeding 15 °C / 27 °F across the heated area) indicate a heater grid problem.

**5.	**Verify the heater overheat protection trips at the expected temperature (verify by reference to 30-31-00 — typical trip is at 95 °C / 203 °F surface).

**6.	**Verify the WINDOW HEAT INOP annunciator does not illuminate during normal operation.

| **NOTE** Acrylic and laminated cockpit windows degrade with age. Crazing (fine surface cracks) is common and is not a defect within the AMM limits. Delamination, on the other hand, is a rejectable defect — a window showing visible delamination at the edge or in the body of the laminate must be removed. Heater grid failures often present as a fault on the controller plus a localized cool spot detectable with the infrared thermometer. |

# **Chapter 13 — Fuel System (ATA 28)**

The 737-800 fuel system consists of two main wing tanks and a center tank, providing a total usable capacity of approximately 6,875 US gallons (26,025 L) of Jet A or Jet A-1. Each main tank is divided by spanwise ribs and contains two boost pumps, a scavenge inlet, a fuel quantity probe array, and a temperature probe. The center tank contains two override pumps and shares a single scavenge inlet with the right main tank. The system also includes the surge tank (the outboard portion of each wing that catches vented fuel), the vent system, the cross-feed plumbing, and the refuel/defuel plumbing.

| **WARNING** Fuel system maintenance is a high-hazard activity. Jet fuel vapors are flammable and explosive in air at concentrations between approximately 0.6 and 4.7 percent by volume; vapor concentrations in a partially fueled tank may be in or near this range. Tank entry requires confined-space entry procedures, calibrated atmospheric monitoring (LEL meter), forced ventilation, intrinsically safe lighting and tools, an attendant outside the tank, and a documented permit. |
| --- |

| **WARNING** Static electricity from clothing, tools, hoses, and personnel can be sufficient to ignite fuel vapor. All fuel-system work requires verified bonding of the aircraft, fuel cart, fuel hose, and personnel before any fuel flow is initiated. Static-dissipative clothing is required for tank entry. |
| --- |

| **CAUTION** Fuel system electrical components in or near the tank (boost pumps, quantity probes, level sensors) are explosion-proof when intact but are NOT explosion-proof if disassembled or improperly reassembled. The integrity of the explosion-proof housings, gaskets, and grounding must be verified after any work that disturbs these components. The fuel-tank ignition prevention requirements (FAR 25.981 / EASA CS 25.981 and associated airworthiness directives) impose specific re-verification steps. |
| --- |

## **13.1 Pre-Refueling Inspection**

- Inspect the refuel/defuel adapter for damage and contamination. Wipe clean before connecting the fuel hose.

- Verify the refuel control panel door operates correctly and the panel placards are legible.

- Verify the refuel panel circuit breakers are set.

- Verify the bonding cable from the fuel cart is connected to the aircraft static ground point on the wing or fuselage before opening the fuel cap or adapter.

- Inspect the wing fuel vent (located at the outboard wingtip) for blockage, ice (in cold conditions), and damage.

- Verify the wheeled fire extinguisher is positioned within the operator-specified distance of the refueling point.

## **13.2 Pressure Refueling Procedure**

**1.	**Position the fuel cart per the operator's procedure. Verify the cart fuel type matches the aircraft requirement (Jet A or Jet A-1).

**2.	**Connect the bonding cable from the cart to the aircraft ground point.

**3.	**Open the refuel panel access door on the lower right wing.

**4.	**Connect the refuel hose to the refuel adapter. Verify the hose nozzle locks fully on the adapter.

**5.	**Open the refuel valve at the panel. Select the destination tank(s) using the tank selector switches.

**6.	**Set the desired fuel quantity on the refuel preselector or monitor the tank quantity indicators directly during the load.

**7.	**Open the fuel cart supply valve. Fuel flow begins. Monitor the tank quantities and listen for unusual sounds.

**8.	**On reaching the preselected quantity (or manual cutoff at the desired quantity), close the cart supply valve.

**9.	**Close the refuel valve at the panel.

**10.	**Disconnect the refuel hose. Cap the adapter and the hose.

**11.	**Disconnect the bonding cable LAST.

**12.	**Verify fuel quantity in each tank agrees with the loaded amount and that fuel imbalance between left and right tanks is within the AMM limit (typically 1,000 lb / 454 kg for ground operations).

**13.	**Record fuel uplift in the aircraft fuel log including grade, density, and quantity.

| **NOTE** Modern refueling systems typically include a precheck function that verifies the high-level shutoff switches in each tank are operating before fueling begins. If the precheck fails, do not attempt to refuel — manual high-level monitoring is not an acceptable substitute. The shutoff switches prevent tank overfill which can damage the vent system and produce a fuel vent overflow. |
| --- |

# **Chapter 14 — Ice and Rain Protection (ATA 30)**

The 737-800 ice and rain protection systems address ice accretion on critical aerodynamic surfaces, on engine inlets, on the pitot-static probes and air-data sensors, and rain on the windshield. The aircraft uses pneumatic anti-icing (engine bleed air directed through leading-edge plenums) on the wing leading-edge slats and engine inlets, electrical heating on the windshield, the pitot probes, the static ports, the angle-of-attack vanes, and the total air temperature probe, and a chemical/mechanical windshield wiper system for rain removal.

| **WARNING** Wing and engine anti-ice systems use high-temperature engine bleed air. Surface temperatures of the slats and engine inlet lip during ground anti-ice testing can exceed 100 °C (212 °F) and will produce burns on contact. Do not approach the wing leading edge or the engine inlet for at least 15 minutes after a ground anti-ice test. |
| --- |

## **14.1 Wing Anti-Ice System**

Wing anti-ice (WAI) on the 737-800 protects the outboard sections of the leading-edge slats (slats 3, 4, 5, and 6 on each wing). Bleed air from the engine pneumatic manifold is routed through a wing anti-ice valve and then through ducting in the slats. The air heats the slat skin and is exhausted overboard through the slat lower surface. Inboard slats (1 and 2) are typically not anti-iced because they operate in the engine inlet flow field and shed ice naturally during normal operation.

### **14.1.1 WAI Operational Check**

**1.	**With APU bleed available or with both engines running at sufficient power, select WING ANTI-ICE to ON.

**2.	**Verify the WAI valve in-transit and open indications on the synoptic display proceed normally.

**3.	**Verify duct pressure indication on the appropriate pneumatic synoptic is within band.

**4.	**After at least 60 seconds of operation, use an infrared thermometer or laser pyrometer to spot-check the slat upper surface temperature at multiple stations along the protected slats. Surface temperature should be rising and approaching the operational range specified in AMM 30-11-00.

**5.	**Verify there is no audible air leakage from the slat ducting (a hissing sound from the slat region indicates duct or plenum leak).

**6.	**Select WING ANTI-ICE to OFF. Verify the WAI valve closes, the synoptic indication returns to normal, and the slat surfaces begin to cool.

### **14.1.2 Wing Anti-Ice Valve Replacement**

## **14.2 Engine Anti-Ice System**

Engine anti-ice (EAI) directs bleed air from the engine into a circumferential plenum behind the engine inlet lip. The heated air maintains the inlet lip and the splitter at temperatures above ice formation in icing conditions. Each engine has its own EAI control valve and is independently selected from the flight deck.

### **14.2.1 EAI Operational Check**

**1.	**With the engine running at idle, select ENGINE ANTI-ICE ON for the affected engine.

**2.	**Verify a small N1 rollback (typical 0.5–1.5 percent) as the bleed air is extracted.

**3.	**Verify the COWL ANTI-ICE indication illuminates on the appropriate display.

**4.	**Verify there is no audible air leakage from the engine inlet lip.

**5.	**After at least 60 seconds, use an infrared thermometer at a safe distance to confirm the inlet lip skin is warming.

**6.	**Select ENGINE ANTI-ICE OFF. Verify the indication clears and the engine returns to its baseline N1.

## **14.3 Windshield Heat**

Windshield heat is provided by transparent conductive coatings (typically a gold-based or indium-tin-oxide film) sandwiched between glass plies. The coating is energized by AC power through bus-bar terminals at the window edges. A controller regulates the current to maintain a target window temperature, which keeps the windshield warm enough to prevent fogging from cabin humidity and ice formation in flight.

Windshield heat troubleshooting is covered in 12.2.3. The most common failures are (1) heater controller failure, presenting as no heat or runaway heating; (2) bus-bar continuity failure, presenting as a no-heat condition; (3) localized coating burn-out, presenting as a cool spot detectable with the infrared thermometer.

## **14.4 Probe Heat**

The pitot probes, the static ports, the angle-of-attack (AOA) vanes, and the total air temperature (TAT) probe are heated to prevent ice formation in flight. Each heater is monitored by a current sensor in the heater circuit. Loss of heat current produces a flight-deck indication.

### **14.4.1 Ground Test of Probe Heaters**

| **WARNING** Probe heaters draw substantial current (typical pitot heater: 200 W; AOA vane heater: 100 W) and reach surface temperatures over 200 °C (392 °F) within seconds. Never touch a probe during or immediately after a heater test. Allow at least 5 minutes of cool-down. Inadvertent touch during a heater test will produce a severe burn. |
| --- |

**1.	**Verify no personnel are within reach of the probes. Post a placard at each probe.

**2.	**Apply electrical power.

**3.	**Set the PROBE HEAT switch to ON (or AUTO with the engines running).

**4.	**Verify the heat-fail annunciators do not illuminate.

**5.	**After at least 30 seconds, observe the probes from a safe distance. Probes should be visibly warm — water droplets on the surface will evaporate quickly.

**6.	**Use an infrared thermometer (held at safe range) to confirm probe surface temperature. Pitot probes typically reach 150–200 °C; AOA vanes 100–150 °C.

**7.	**Verify the heater current draw on the affected bus is within band (check at the bus monitor or by clamp-on ammeter at the appropriate point in the wiring).

**8.	**Set the PROBE HEAT switch to OFF. Allow at least 5 minutes before any probe is touched.

## **14.5 Windshield Wiper System**

The 737-800 has two windshield wipers, one for each forward windshield. Each wiper is driven by an electric motor mounted in the structure adjacent to the windshield. Operating speeds are LOW, HIGH, and an INT (intermittent) mode in some configurations. Wiper arms include a tension adjustment and a parking position.

### **14.5.1 Wiper Blade Replacement**

**1.	**Lift the wiper arm fully off the windshield.

**2.	**Press the blade release tab and slide the blade off the arm hook.

**3.	**Install the new blade onto the arm hook. Verify the blade locks in place.

**4.	**Carefully lower the arm onto the windshield, ensuring the blade contacts evenly across its full length.

**5.	**Cycle the wiper on a wet windshield (a controlled hose flow or rain) to verify clean wipe with no streaking or chatter.

### **14.5.2 Wiper Motor Replacement**

**1.	**Apply LOTO to the wiper motor circuit breaker.

**2.	**Remove the wiper arm by loosening the arm-to-shaft nut at the base of the arm.

**3.	**Open the maintenance access in the cockpit structure to expose the motor.

**4.	**Disconnect the motor electrical connector.

**5.	**Remove the motor mounting bolts and lift the motor clear.

**6.	**Install the replacement motor. Verify the motor output shaft aligns properly with the structural shaft seat.

**7.	**Connect the electrical harness. Verify proper seating.

**8.	**Reinstall the wiper arm. Confirm the arm is at the correct parked angle relative to the windshield reference mark.

**9.	**Remove LOTO. Perform an operational check at LOW and HIGH speeds, and verify the wiper returns to the parked position when switched OFF.

# **Chapter 15 — Auxiliary Power Unit (ATA 49)**

The 737-800 auxiliary power unit (APU) is a small gas-turbine engine mounted in the tail cone of the aircraft. The APU provides electrical power (through its 90 kVA generator), pneumatic power (through bleed-air extraction), and is used for engine starting, ground operation without ground service equipment, and as a backup electrical source in flight. Typical equipment is the Honeywell 131-9B or similar APU model.

APU maintenance includes routine servicing (oil and filter), inspection (compressor and turbine borescope, intake), and on-condition removal. Major work on the APU is generally performed by APU OEM-authorized shops; on-aircraft maintenance is limited to routine servicing, troubleshooting, and complete LRU removal.

| **WARNING** APU exhaust gas reaches temperatures over 500 °C during operation and the tail-cone area remains hot for an extended period after shutdown. The APU air-intake door is mechanically driven and may move without warning if the system is energized. Maintain a clearance of at least 10 feet from the APU exhaust and inlet during APU operations, and apply LOTO to the APU control during maintenance. |
| --- |

## **15.1 APU Oil Servicing**

**Reference Information — APU Oil Servicing**

| **Item** | **Detail** |
| --- | --- |
| Component | Auxiliary Power Unit |
| Approved Oil | BMS 3-24 Type II turbine oil, 5 cSt grade |
| Oil Capacity (approximate) | 2.4 US qt (2.3 L) |
| ATA Reference | 49-91-01 |
| Approximate Time | 0.5 manhours |

**1.	**Shut down the APU. Allow at least 10 minutes for the oil to drain back to the sump and the surfaces to cool sufficiently to permit work.

**2.	**Open the APU access panel on the lower aft fuselage.

**3.	**Locate the oil level sight glass on the APU accessory pad.

**4.	**Read the sight glass. The acceptable range is between the two index marks. If at or below the lower mark, top up.

**5.	**Add oil through the fill port in small increments (0.1 qt / 0.1 L), verifying the level reading after each addition. Do not overfill.

**6.	**Close the fill port. Torque the fill cap to the value on the cap (typical 25–30 in-lbf).

**7.	**Wipe any oil residue and inspect for visible leakage.

**8.	**Close the access panel.

**9.	**Make AML entry showing quantity added, batch number, and date.

## **15.2 APU Inlet Inspection**

The APU inlet door modulates open during APU operation and is fully closed when the APU is off. Inspect the inlet for FOD, ice damage, bird strike, and door mechanism condition during scheduled maintenance and after any event that may have introduced inlet contamination.

- Inspect the inlet door surface for dents, cracks, missing fasteners, or seal damage.

- Inspect the inlet duct interior for FOD, evidence of bird remains, ice damage, or skin damage.

- Verify the inlet door operates smoothly through its full travel using the maintenance test function.

- Inspect the door actuator and linkage for wear, corrosion, and proper lubrication.

- Inspect the door perimeter seal for tears, set, and contamination.

## **15.3 APU Borescope Inspection**

APU borescope inspection is typically scheduled at intervals of 1,500–3,000 APU operating hours, with additional triggered inspections after specific events (over-temperature, surge, FOD ingestion). The inspection accesses the compressor stages, the combustor, and the turbine stages through purpose-built borescope ports on the APU case.

| **WARNING** APU borescope inspection requires the unit to be fully cooled (typical 2 hours minimum after shutdown) and isolated from any starting source. Apply LOTO to the APU start switch and the APU fuel shutoff. Never attempt borescope inspection on a recently-run APU. |
| --- |

**1.	**Confirm the APU has cooled for at least 2 hours.

**2.	**Apply LOTO.

**3.	**Identify the borescope ports per the APU CMM.

**4.	**Remove the borescope port plug. The plug typically is a small threaded plug with a copper or aluminum washer.

**5.	**Insert the borescope probe slowly. Do not force the probe into a tight bend.

**6.	**Inspect each accessible blade or vane for cracks, leading-edge erosion, tip rub, foreign-object damage, and burning. Document with photo or video as supported by the borescope system.

**7.	**Compare findings against the APU CMM acceptance criteria. Defects exceeding the limits drive a maintenance disposition — typically blending, replacement, or removal of the APU.

**8.	**Withdraw the probe carefully.

**9.	**Install a new sealing washer on the borescope port plug. Reinstall the plug and torque per the CMM.

**10.	**Remove LOTO. Following a borescope inspection, the APU is typically run for at least 5 minutes to verify no leakage at the borescope ports.

## **15.4 APU Test Run**

An APU test run is performed after major maintenance (oil change with filter replacement, borescope, accessory replacement) and before returning the APU to service. The test verifies start performance, steady-state operation, generator output, bleed air output, and shutdown sequence.

**1.	**Confirm aircraft is positioned with the exhaust away from buildings, other aircraft, and ground equipment. Confirm the area aft of the aircraft is clear within the operator-specified distance.

**2.	**Confirm fire bottles are full and the fire detection system is functioning. Wheeled fire extinguisher in position.

**3.	**Confirm fuel level is sufficient for the planned test plus reserve.

**4.	**Start the APU per the standard start procedure. Time the start: typical start time is 60–90 seconds from start switch ON to ready signal.

**5.	**Record EGT (exhaust gas temperature) peak during start. Peak EGT must be within the APU CMM start limit.

**6.	**Allow the APU to stabilize for at least 2 minutes.

**7.	**Verify generator output: voltage 115 V (±2 V), frequency 400 Hz (±4 Hz).

**8.	**Verify bleed air output: pressure within band for the APU model (typically 35–50 psig at the bleed port at the test conditions).

**9.	**Operate the APU for at least 10 minutes at steady state. Monitor for unusual vibrations, oil leaks, exhaust smoke, or alarm indications.

**10.	**Shut down the APU. Verify orderly run-down. The APU run-down (from shutdown signal to N=0) is typically less than 60 seconds; a long run-down or hung run-down indicates bearing distress or an air leak.

**11.	**Inspect for oil leaks, fuel leaks, or external fuel staining.

**12.	**Make AML entry: start time, peak EGT, steady-state EGT, generator output, bleed output, total run time.

| **NOTE** APU start performance trending is an important reliability indicator. Operators typically track start time, peak EGT, and ITT margin over time. A gradual rise in start time or peak EGT (when normalized for ambient conditions) is an early sign of compressor degradation or turbine wear. The APU OEM provides the trending guidance and the action thresholds. |

# **Chapter 16 — Inspection Programs and Maintenance Check Intervals (ATA 05)**

This chapter summarizes the scheduled-maintenance philosophy applied to the 737-800 airframe under the synthetic operator program referenced by this manual. Scheduled tasks are derived from the Maintenance Review Board (MRB) report, the Maintenance Planning Document (MPD), and the operator's own Continuous Airworthiness Maintenance Program (CAMP) as approved by the regulator. Tasks are categorized by interval driver (calendar time, flight hours, flight cycles, or special events) and by the level of disassembly required.

| **NOTE** All intervals in this chapter are synthetic and illustrative. Real operator intervals are governed by the operator's approved maintenance program, the manufacturer's MPD revisions, and the regulatory authority. Always defer to the operator's current task card and the approved data. |
| --- |

## **16.1 Check Letter Definitions**

The historic A/B/C/D check letter system has been largely replaced under MSG-3 logic by interval-driven task packages, but the letter designations remain in common operational use as convenient shorthand for the depth of the maintenance event.

### **Table 16-1 — Maintenance Check Categories (Synthetic Intervals)**

| **Check** | **Typical Interval** | **Approximate Duration** | **Scope Summary** |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Transit (TR) | Every flight | 10–30 minutes | Walk-around inspection, fluid level top-off, tire pressure spot-check, no-go item verification. |
| Daily (DY) | Every 24–48 hours | 1–2 hours | Expanded walk-around, engine oil servicing, hydraulic reservoir check, brake wear check, tire/wheel inspection. |
| Weekly (WK) | Every 7 days | 2–4 hours | Items from daily plus interior check, emergency equipment verification, additional fluid sampling. |
| A-Check | Approximately 600–800 FH | 1–2 days | Light maintenance, lubrication, filter changes, operational checks, minor system tests. Aircraft typically remains in service overnight. |
| C-Check | Approximately 20–24 months | 2–4 weeks | Heavy maintenance, extensive inspection of structure and systems, partial cabin removal, NDT inspections, corrosion control. |
| Heavy Check (D or 6Y) | Approximately 6–10 years | 4–8 weeks | Heaviest scheduled visit. Substantial disassembly, structural inspections, major component overhauls, painting, full cabin refurbishment. |

## **16.2 Transit Check Task List**

The transit check is performed by line maintenance personnel or by qualified crew at each turnaround. It is intended to detect obvious damage, fluid leaks, missing components, or other discrepancies that would affect dispatch.

### **16.2.1 Exterior Walk-Around**

**1.	**Begin at the nose. Inspect the radome for hail damage, lightning strike marks (small pin-holes or burn marks), and security of the radome latches.

**2.	**Inspect the nose gear: tire condition and inflation indication, strut extension, drag brace, steering linkage, gear door alignment, taxi light and runway turnoff light condition.

**3.	**Inspect the forward fuselage: pitot-static probes (covers removed, no obstruction), angle-of-attack vanes (free movement), TAT probe, lower antennas (no damage, no missing fasteners), forward cargo door (closed and locked, seal condition).

**4.	**Inspect the left wing leading edge: slat condition, no fuel weeping at access panels, navigation light condition, wing-root area for fluid leaks.

**5.	**Inspect the left engine: fan blade condition (visual rotation of fan and inspection of all visible blades for nicks, dents, or curling of the leading edge), inlet lip for hail or bird damage, exhaust plug and nozzle, no oil streaking on the cowling, fan cowl latches secure.

**6.	**Inspect the left main gear: tire condition, wear indicators, brake wear pin extension, hydraulic line condition (no weeping or drip marks on tarmac under the gear), torque link condition.

**7.	**Inspect the left wing trailing edge: flap and aileron condition, no hydraulic fluid weeping at actuators, static wicks present (count and condition).

**8.	**Inspect the empennage: horizontal stabilizer condition, elevator condition, vertical fin and rudder condition, APU exhaust area (no oil staining beyond normal), tail navigation light, tail skid clearance.

**9.	**Inspect the right wing trailing edge, right main gear, right engine, right wing leading edge — mirror image of steps 4–7.

**10.	**Return to the forward fuselage on the right side and complete the loop. Verify all access panels are closed and flush, all service panel doors are latched, and no ground equipment, tools, or debris remain near the aircraft.

### **16.2.2 Transit Check Pass/Fail Items**

The following conditions discovered during a transit check are dispatch-affecting and must be cleared or properly deferred via the MEL before the next flight.

- Tire wear beyond the limit specified in Chapter 5 (cord exposed, sidewall cuts, flat spots beyond limits).

- Hydraulic fluid leak — any drip rate from a wheel well, flight control, or thrust reverser hydraulic component.

- Fuel leak — any active drip or wet area on a tank access panel or fuel line fitting.

- Engine fan blade damage — any nick, dent, or tear in a fan blade leading edge greater than the dimensions allowed by Chapter 6.

- Inlet lip damage — any hail dent, bird impact, or crack visible on the engine inlet lip.

- Static wicks — more than two wicks missing from any single flight control surface, or any wick missing from the rudder trailing edge.

- Pitot-static probe obstruction — any visible obstruction, insect debris, or damage to a pitot probe or static port.

- Cabin emergency equipment — any missing or seal-broken emergency equipment item (life vests, oxygen bottles, first-aid kits, fire extinguishers).

## **16.3 A-Check Task Package Overview**

A typical A-check at the synthetic operator described by this manual occurs at intervals of approximately 600 flight hours, with calendar limits not to exceed four months between checks. The task package consists of approximately 80–120 individual task cards depending on the specific A-check sequence number (A1 through A8 in the operator's program, cycling back to A1 thereafter).

### **16.3.1 Representative A-Check Tasks**

- Engine oil sample for spectrographic analysis (both engines and APU).

- Hydraulic fluid sample for particle count analysis (System A, System B, Standby).

- Main wheel bearing inspection and re-greasing on every fourth A-check (A4, A8).

- Air filter replacement on the air-conditioning packs.

- Operational check of the cargo fire detection and suppression system.

- Operational check of all emergency lighting (cabin floor path lights, exit signs, exterior emergency lights).

- Operational check of the passenger oxygen system manual deploy.

- Detailed inspection of all main and nose landing gear tires for wear, cuts, embedded objects, and inflation pressure recorded against the chart in Chapter 5.

- Brake wear pin measurement on all main wheel brakes, with replacement decision per the wear chart.

- Visual inspection of the engine pylon area for fluid leaks (fuel, oil, hydraulic) and for any heat damage or burned wiring.

- Functional check of the autobrake system on jacks (lower-tier A-checks may defer this to a higher-tier check).

- Battery capacity check on the main and APU batteries; replacement if capacity has fallen below 85 percent of rated.

- Inspection and lubrication of the flight control cable runs, pulley brackets, and bellcranks per the lubrication chart in Chapter 7.

- Galley and lavatory inspection for water system leaks, drain ball-valve operation, and waste tank quick-disconnect condition.

## **16.4 C-Check Task Package Overview**

The C-check is the first level of heavy maintenance and typically requires the aircraft to be out of service for two to four weeks at a maintenance facility equipped to perform extensive inspections, structural repairs, and component changes. C-checks at this synthetic operator occur on a 20-month calendar interval, irrespective of utilization, with a 24-month maximum interval permitted under approved program flexibility.

### **16.4.1 Representative C-Check Tasks**

- Removal of selected cabin interior panels and floor panels for inspection of the underlying structure, wiring, and ducting.

- Detailed visual inspection of the wing-to-fuselage attachment fittings, including fluorescent penetrant inspection on selected fitting lugs.

- Detailed visual and NDT inspection of the horizontal stabilizer attachment and the trim actuator gimbal.

- Inspection of the pressure bulkheads (forward and aft) for cracking, corrosion, and fastener security.

- Inspection of all door surrounds, door corner doublers, and door stop fittings.

- Inspection of the lower fuselage and keel beam area for corrosion (a common high-attention area on aged 737s).

- Detailed inspection of all landing gear components, including removal of the wheels and brakes for shop inspection.

- Functional and leak check of the entire fuel system at maximum operating pressure.

- Borescope inspection of both engine hot sections per the engine manufacturer's interval.

- Replacement of all life-limited rotating engine components that will reach their cycle limit before the next planned C-check.

- Compass swing and IRS alignment verification on a properly surveyed compass rose after the check.

- Weighing of the aircraft if required by the operator's program (typical interval is every second or third C-check, or after major modification).

## **16.5 Corrosion Prevention and Control Program**

Boeing has issued a Corrosion Prevention and Control Program (CPCP) document for the 737NG family that defines specific structural areas to be inspected, the level of inspection required, the corrosion-protective coatings to be applied or restored, and the reporting requirements when corrosion is found. This synthetic operator incorporates the CPCP requirements into the C-check task package and tracks corrosion findings against the rating scale (Level 1, Level 2, Level 3) defined in the CPCP.

### **Table 16-2 — Corrosion Severity Levels (Synthetic, Modeled on Standard CPCP)**

| **Level** | **Definition** | **Operator Action Required** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Level 1 | Corrosion occurring between successive inspections of an area, where the damage does not exceed the allowable limits of the SRM (Structural Repair Manual). Removable by light blending; structure remains within limits. | Blend out, restore protective finish, record finding. No reporting to manufacturer required, but operator tracks for trend analysis. |
| Level 2 | Corrosion occurring between successive inspections that exceeds Level 1, requires a repair beyond a simple blend-out, but does not result in immediate operating restriction. | Repair per SRM. Report to manufacturer per the CPCP procedure. Operator should evaluate inspection interval for that zone for possible reduction. |
| Level 3 | Corrosion found during the first or any subsequent inspection that is determined to be an urgent airworthiness concern requiring expedited action. | Repair before further flight, or operator obtains specific concession from regulator. Report to manufacturer and to regulator. Trigger fleet-wide assessment of the affected zone. |

| **CAUTION** Findings classified at Level 2 or above require timely reporting through the operator's continuing airworthiness organization to the manufacturer and, in some cases, to the regulator. Late reporting can result in a finding against the operator at the next regulatory audit. |
| --- |

## **16.6 Aging Aircraft Inspections**

Once an airframe accumulates beyond a defined threshold of flight cycles or calendar years (the threshold depends on the model and the regulator, but for the 737NG is commonly applied beginning around 60,000 cycles or 20 calendar years), additional inspections under the Supplemental Inspection Document (SID) program are required. These inspections target known fatigue-critical and corrosion-prone areas identified through service experience and fleet-wide analysis.

- Lap joint inspections — high-time 737 airframes have historically shown fatigue cracking at certain longitudinal fuselage lap joints. SID tasks include detailed visual and eddy-current inspections of specified joints.

- Fuselage skin doubler inspections — areas where doublers have been installed for previous repairs or modifications receive elevated inspection scrutiny.

- Wing rear-spar lower-chord inspections — fatigue-critical location requiring NDT at the specified interval.

- Empennage attachment inspections — horizontal stabilizer pivot fittings and vertical fin attachment fittings receive expanded inspection.

- Fuel-tank wiring inspections per the Fuel Tank Safety rule, including verification of bonding and the absence of chafing on any wiring routed within or adjacent to a fuel tank.

## **16.7 Maintenance Record Keeping**

All maintenance performed on the aircraft must be entered into the appropriate record. The operator maintains separate records for the airframe, each engine, the APU, the landing gear assemblies, the propeller assemblies (where applicable), and each life-limited component. Records are retained per the regulatory requirement (typically the life of the component for life-limited records, and a defined period for routine maintenance records).

### **16.7.1 Required Entries**

- Description of work performed, with reference to the approved data used (AMM task number, SRM reference, EA reference, or service bulletin number).

- Aircraft hours and cycles at the time of the work.

- Date of completion.

- Identification of the certifying staff (name, certification number, and signature or electronic-equivalent authentication).

- Identification of any components removed and installed (part number, serial number, modification status, life-limit status).

- Reference to the airworthiness release statement signed for the work.

| **CAUTION** Maintenance records are legal airworthiness documents. Falsification of records is a criminal offence in most jurisdictions and is grounds for immediate loss of certification for the individuals involved and for the certificating organization. Records must be created at the time of the work, not reconstructed afterward. |
